The difference in sale price has less to do with app quality than with preparation quality.

Two Rork apps both generating $400/month in net profit can sell for dramatically different amounts. The unprepared one might fetch $4,000 — 10x monthly profit. The one whose owner spent six months deliberately improving its metrics and documentation might sell for $12,000 — 30x. That gap isn't luck. It's the result of understanding what buyers are actually evaluating and systematically addressing it.

Having worked through multiple app sales and portfolio management decisions, the pattern I've seen most often is this: indie developers list their apps before they're "acquisition-ready," and they leave money on the table as a result. This article is a playbook to avoid that mistake.

## Why Preparation Quality Determines Sale Price

Buyers conducting due diligence are fundamentally doing risk assessment. Their core fear is that revenue collapses after the sale. Every question they ask is oriented around that single concern.

An app with opaque metrics or missing data forces buyers to build a risk premium into their offer — they discount the price to compensate for what they can't see. An app that anticipates every due diligence question and answers it with clear evidence gives buyers confidence. Confident buyers pay higher multiples.

Preparing to sell means building a body of evidence that makes the risk feel manageable. That's it.

## Step 1 — Audit Your App Through the Buyer's Eyes

Before starting any improvements, score your current state honestly. This scoring system maps directly to what buyers weigh in due diligence.

```python
# App acquisition readiness scoring system
# Run this before listing to identify your highest-leverage improvements

@dataclass
class AppAuditResult:
    score: int
    max_score: int
    issues: list[str]
    recommended_multiple_range: tuple[int, int]

def audit_app_for_sale(
    revenue_trend: str,           # "growing" / "stable" / "declining"
    monthly_variance_pct: float,  # e.g., 0.12 = 12% variance
    revenue_source_count: int,    # 1=ads only, 2=ads+IAP, 3=ads+IAP+sub
    owner_hours_per_month: float,
    day30_retention_pct: float,
    store_rating: float,
    months_of_data: int,
) -> AppAuditResult:

    score = 0
    issues = []

    # Revenue stability (30 points)
    trend_score = {"growing": 30, "stable": 20, "declining": 5}
    score += trend_score.get(revenue_trend, 5)
    if revenue_trend == "declining":
        issues.append("CRITICAL: Declining revenue severely compresses multiples. Reverse trend before listing.")

    if monthly_variance_pct <= 0.10:
        pass  # Good — no penalty
    elif monthly_variance_pct <= 0.20:
        issues.append(f"Monthly variance {monthly_variance_pct*100:.0f}% is moderate — subscription revenue would stabilize this")
    else:
        score -= 5
        issues.append(f"Monthly variance {monthly_variance_pct*100:.0f}% is high — buyers will discount for unpredictability")

    source_bonus = {1: 0, 2: 10, 3: 20}
    score += source_bonus.get(revenue_source_count, 0)
    if revenue_source_count == 1:
        issues.append("Single revenue source: buyers see this as a single point of failure. Adding IAP or subscription raises multiples significantly.")

    # Business continuity (25 points)
    if owner_hours_per_month <= 2:
        score += 25
    elif owner_hours_per_month <= 8:
        score += 15
        issues.append(f"{owner_hours_per_month}h/month owner time: automate monitoring to reduce this below 2h")
    else:
        score += 5
        issues.append(f"{owner_hours_per_month}h/month owner time: buyers see this as a job, not an asset. Automation is essential.")

    # Growth indicators (25 points)
    if day30_retention_pct >= 0.15:
        score += 15
    elif day30_retention_pct >= 0.10:
        score += 8
        issues.append(f"Day-30 retention {day30_retention_pct*100:.0f}%: push notifications or onboarding improvement could raise this")
    else:
        issues.append(f"Day-30 retention {day30_retention_pct*100:.0f}%: below industry average. Focus on engagement before listing.")

    if store_rating >= 4.2:
        score += 10
    elif store_rating >= 3.8:
        score += 5
        issues.append(f"Rating {store_rating:.1f}: review prompt optimization could push this above 4.2")
    else:
        issues.append(f"Rating {store_rating:.1f}: ratings below 4.0 are a significant red flag for buyers")

    # Data maturity
    if months_of_data >= 18:
        score += 0  # Already reflected in stability scores
    elif months_of_data < 12:
        issues.append(f"Only {months_of_data} months of data: buyers prefer 12+ months of history")

    # Translate score to multiple estimate
    if score >= 80:
        multiple_range = (28, 36)
    elif score >= 65:
        multiple_range = (20, 27)
    elif score >= 50:
        multiple_range = (14, 19)
    else:
        multiple_range = (8, 13)

    return AppAuditResult(
        score=score, max_score=100,
        issues=issues,
        recommended_multiple_range=multiple_range
    )
```

A score above 70 means you're close to listing-ready. Below 50, focus on the issues list before considering a sale.

## Step 2 — Diversify Revenue to Structurally Raise Your Multiple

The single highest-leverage improvement most indie apps can make is adding a second or third revenue source. Market data shows a clear pattern:

| Revenue Structure | Typical Multiple Range |
|---|---|
| Ads only | 10–15x |
| Ads + IAP | 18–24x |
| Ads + IAP + Subscription | 25–36x |
| Subscription-focused (no ads) | 28–40x |

The mechanics are straightforward: subscription revenue is recurring and predictable, which is exactly what buyers want to see. Even if subscriptions represent only 20% of your total revenue, they improve the perceived stability of the whole.

For Rork apps, adding RevenueCat-powered subscriptions alongside existing AdMob revenue is the fastest path to this structure:

```javascript
// RevenueCat integration pattern for adding subscriptions to an ad-supported Rork app
// Goal: "Free with ads" + "Premium subscription (no ads + extra features)"

import Purchases, { LOG_LEVEL } from 'react-native-purchases';

  await Purchases.configure({
    apiKey: Platform.OS === 'ios'
      ? process.env.EXPO_PUBLIC_RC_IOS_KEY
      : process.env.EXPO_PUBLIC_RC_ANDROID_KEY,
  });
};

// Check premium status — call this wherever you need to gate features
export const checkPremiumStatus = async (): Promise<boolean> => {
  try {
    const customerInfo = await Purchases.getCustomerInfo();
    return Object.keys(customerInfo.entitlements.active).includes('premium');
  } catch {
    return false; // Fail safe: treat errors as non-premium
  }
};

// Show paywall at the right moment — not immediately on launch
export const offerUpgradeIfAppropriate = async (
  sessionCount: number,
  hasTriggeredCoreFeature: boolean
) => {
  // Wait until user has experienced value before showing paywall
  if (sessionCount < 3 || !hasTriggeredCoreFeature) return;
  
  const isPremium = await checkPremiumStatus();
  if (!isPremium) {
    // RevenueCat's built-in paywall handles the purchase flow
    const result = await Purchases.presentPaywallIfNeeded('premium');
    console.log('Paywall result:', result);
  }
};

// Listen for purchase updates
Purchases.addCustomerInfoUpdateListener((customerInfo) => {
  const isPremium = Object.keys(customerInfo.entitlements.active).includes('premium');
  // Update your app state — hide ads, enable features
  updatePremiumState(isPremium);
});
```

Operate this structure for at least 3 months — ideally 6 — before listing. Buyers will ask for RevenueCat dashboard screenshots showing subscription MRR and churn rate. Having 6 months of subscription data is a meaningful differentiator.

## Step 3 — Improve the Four Metrics Buyers Always Verify

Four metrics appear in virtually every app due diligence process. Here's how to move each one before listing.

### Day-30 Retention

This metric tells buyers whether your users find lasting value. The industry average for consumer apps is around 10–15% Day-30 retention; anything above 15% is strong.

The fastest improvement: a well-designed push notification sequence for new users. Not spam — a genuinely useful re-engagement flow timed around the moments users are most likely to have forgotten the app exists.

```javascript
// Retention-focused notification sequence using Expo Notifications
import * as Notifications from 'expo-notifications';
import AsyncStorage from '@react-native-async-storage/async-storage';

export const scheduleRetentionSequence = async () => {
  // Only schedule once per install
  const alreadyScheduled = await AsyncStorage.getItem('retention_notifs_scheduled');
  if (alreadyScheduled) return;

  // Day 3: Remind them of the core value proposition
  await Notifications.scheduleNotificationAsync({
    content: {
      title: "Quick check-in",
      body: "You haven't tried [core feature] yet — it takes 30 seconds",
    },
    trigger: { seconds: 3 * 24 * 60 * 60 },
  });

  // Day 7: Deliver a concrete tip or piece of value
  await Notifications.scheduleNotificationAsync({
    content: {
      title: "Tip of the week",
      body: "Did you know you can [specific useful action]? Most users miss this.",
    },
    trigger: { seconds: 7 * 24 * 60 * 60 },
  });

  // Day 14: Social proof or progress nudge
  await Notifications.scheduleNotificationAsync({
    content: {
      title: "You're ahead of most users",
      body: "People who use [app] for 2 weeks typically [positive outcome]",
    },
    trigger: { seconds: 14 * 24 * 60 * 60 },
  });

  await AsyncStorage.setItem('retention_notifs_scheduled', 'true');
};
```

### Store Rating Average

A 4.0+ rating is the minimum threshold buyers won't question. Below 4.0, expect pushback. Above 4.3, it actively supports higher multiples.

The most effective single change: prompt for reviews at the right moment. Not immediately on launch — after the user has experienced the core value.

```javascript
import * as StoreReview from 'expo-store-review';

export const requestReviewIfAppropriate = async (userState: {
  sessionCount: number;
  hasCompletedCoreFlow: boolean;
  lastPromptDate: Date | null;
}) => {
  const daysSinceLastPrompt = userState.lastPromptDate
    ? (Date.now() - userState.lastPromptDate.getTime()) / (1000 * 60 * 60 * 24)
    : Infinity;

  const shouldPrompt =
    userState.sessionCount >= 5 &&
    userState.hasCompletedCoreFlow &&
    daysSinceLastPrompt >= 30; // Don't pester

  if (shouldPrompt && (await StoreReview.hasAction())) {
    await StoreReview.requestReview();
    await saveLastPromptDate(new Date());
  }
};
```

### Monthly Revenue Variance

Variance above 20% month-over-month signals unpredictability. Buyers discount for this. The structural fix — as noted in Step 2 — is adding subscription revenue, which is inherently low-variance.

If subscription revenue isn't feasible immediately, look at whether your ad revenue variance is driven by specific seasonal keywords or by traffic spikes from organic discovery. Diversifying your app's keyword targeting across multiple user intents can smooth this out over time.

### Owner Time Per Month

"How many hours per month does this require?" is asked in every due diligence call. More than 10 hours per month makes buyers nervous. More than 40 hours makes them walk away.

Automate your monitoring stack before listing:

```python
# Automated monitoring with GitHub Actions — reduce owner time to near zero

# .github/workflows/weekly_health_check.yml
# Runs every Monday at 9am JST

import os
import json
import requests
from datetime import datetime, timedelta

class AppHealthMonitor:
    def __init__(self):
        self.slack_webhook = os.environ["SLACK_WEBHOOK_URL"]
        self.admob_refresh_token = os.environ["ADMOB_REFRESH_TOKEN"]

    def check_revenue_anomaly(self, daily_revenue: list[float]) -> bool:
        """Flag if today's revenue is >30% below the 7-day average"""
        if len(daily_revenue) < 7:
            return False
        avg = sum(daily_revenue[-7:]) / 7
        latest = daily_revenue[-1]
        return latest < avg * 0.7

    def send_alert(self, message: str, urgent: bool = False):
        emoji = "🚨" if urgent else "📊"
        requests.post(self.slack_webhook, json={
            "text": f"{emoji} App Health: {message}"
        })

    def weekly_report(self, revenue_data: dict, crash_data: dict):
        report_lines = [
            f"Weekly Health Check — {datetime.now().strftime('%Y-%m-%d')}",
            f"Revenue (7d): ${revenue_data['weekly_total']:.2f}",
            f"vs Prior Week: {revenue_data['wow_change']:+.1%}",
            f"Crash-free rate: {crash_data['crash_free_pct']:.1%}",
        ]
        if crash_data['crash_free_pct'] < 0.995:
            self.send_alert("\n".join(report_lines), urgent=True)
        else:
            self.send_alert("\n".join(report_lines), urgent=False)
```

With automated monitoring sending you a weekly Slack report and urgent alerts on anomalies, your actual weekly time commitment drops to reading one message and acting only if something flags red.

Host this in a shared Google Drive or Dropbox folder. When a serious buyer makes contact, you send one link and they have everything they need. Buyers who have to chase you for documents often lose confidence and move on.

## Step 5 — Understand Sale Timing

When you list matters almost as much as how you list.

**Strong listing conditions:**
- Revenue is up 10%+ compared to the same period last year
- You've shipped a meaningful update in the past 3 months (shows the app isn't abandoned)
- Store rating is at or near its all-time high
- You have 18+ months of data to show

**Conditions worth waiting out:**
- Revenue has declined for 3+ consecutive months
- A major bug or App Store rejection happened in the past 30 days
- A key third-party SDK you depend on is being deprecated and you haven't migrated yet

Seasonality also plays a role. Buyer activity on Flippa and Acquire.com tends to peak in January (new-year budget cycles) and September (pre-fiscal-year-end). December is generally the slowest month — buyers are distracted and decision-making slows.

## Step 6 — LOI to Close: Negotiation Tactics That Hold Your Price

When a Letter of Intent arrives, resist the urge to respond immediately with acceptance. Here's how to navigate this phase:

**Anchoring and counter-offers**

List at 10–15% above your target price. Buyers will negotiate down; you want room to meet them at your actual target and make them feel they won a concession.

When the first offer comes in below your listing price, don't counter immediately. Wait 24 hours. This signals that you're not desperate to sell and gives you time to evaluate. If you have multiple interested buyers (visible on Flippa via the watchlist counter), mention that: "I'm in conversations with a couple of other interested parties" is factually accurate and creates appropriate urgency.

**Terms to accept, negotiate, and reject**

```
✅ Accept without hesitation
   • Escrow via Flippa's built-in service or Escrow.com (always use escrow)
   • 30-day post-close support period (this is standard and low-effort)
   • Read-only App Store Connect access during due diligence

🔄 Negotiate carefully
   • Earnout clauses: If the buyer wants 20–30% of the price tied to 
     3-month post-close revenue targets, this can be reasonable — 
     but only if you'll retain enough operational involvement to 
     influence the outcome. Above 40% earnout, the risk is too high.
   • Non-compete scope: Time-limited and category-specific non-competes 
     (e.g., "no new apps in the [meditation] category for 12 months") 
     are common. Broad non-competes spanning all app categories for 3+ 
     years should be negotiated down.

❌ Walk away from
   • Full payment outside escrow ("I'll send you crypto directly")
   • Admin access to App Store Connect or developer accounts before payment clears
   • Verbal agreements to transfer after "a small initial payment"
```

**Due diligence period**

Grant buyers 7–14 days for due diligence. Give them read-only access to App Store Connect and your ad network dashboards — not admin access. If they ask for source code access during DD (before payment), provide it only under a mutual NDA that prohibits them from copying or using the code if the deal doesn't close.

## After the Sale: What Happens in the First 30 Days

The 30-day support period post-close is usually lighter than sellers expect. The main activities:

1. **Ad network account migration**: Help the buyer set up their AdMob/ad network account and update the app's ad unit IDs. This typically requires a minor app update.

2. **RevenueCat migration**: Transfer the RevenueCat project to the buyer's account. RevenueCat's support team handles this and it's straightforward.

3. **Analytics handoff**: Grant the buyer access to Firebase/Mixpanel/your analytics platform and walk them through the key dashboards.

4. **First update**: The buyer may want to push a minor update to verify they have the full development → submission → release pipeline working. This is reasonable to support.

After 30 days, your obligation ends. Most transitions go smoothly because buyers who get to this stage are motivated to make the app succeed.

## Building With the Exit in Mind

The insight that changes how you approach Rork development: apps that are worth a lot at exit are apps that run well without you. Automated operations, stable revenue across multiple sources, healthy user retention, good store ratings — these are both what makes an app valuable to buyers and what makes it satisfying to own.

Build your next Rork project with the 3-year horizon in mind. Not because you'll definitely sell it, but because building something that *could* be sold for 30x monthly profit at any point you choose is a fundamentally different — and better — way to think about indie development.
