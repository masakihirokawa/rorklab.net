# The Complete Guide to Monetizing Your Rork App

Once you've built a great app with Rork, the next step is **monetization**. No matter how good your app is, sustainable revenue is what makes long-term development and improvement possible.

This guide covers the three major monetization models for mobile apps — subscriptions, ad revenue, and in-app purchases — with practical strategies you can apply to your Rork projects.

---

## 1. Choosing a Revenue Model

### Comparing the Three Core Models

Before anything else, match your revenue model to the nature of your app.

**Subscriptions (monthly or annual)**
Best for apps that deliver ongoing value. Most effective when users return daily or weekly.

- Best for: productivity tools, learning apps, fitness, meditation, news, streaming
- Revenue stability: High (predictable MRR)
- Psychological barrier for users: Moderate (ongoing commitment)

**Ad revenue (AdMob or ad networks)**
Acquire users for free, then monetize through ad impressions. Works well for casual games, utilities, and content browsing apps.

- Best for: games, wallpapers, utilities, content browsing
- Revenue stability: Moderate (tied to DAU)
- Psychological barrier for users: Low (the app is free)

**In-app purchases (one-time or consumables)**
Users buy specific features, content, or items in one-time transactions. Common in games and creative tools.

- Best for: games, creative tools, educational content
- Revenue stability: Low (depends on purchase timing)
- Psychological barrier for users: Low to moderate (clear value exchange)

### Designing a Hybrid Model

Most successful apps combine multiple models. Common combinations include:

- **Freemium + subscription**: Core features are free; advanced features require a subscription
- **Ads + ad removal purchase**: Free users see ads; paid users get an ad-free experience
- **Freemium + consumables**: Base gameplay is free; powerful items or extra lives are purchasable

## 2. Designing and Implementing Subscriptions

### How to Think About Pricing

Subscription pricing comes down to a single question: are you delivering enough ongoing value that users want to keep paying?

**Using a Price Sensitivity Meter (PSM)**
Survey your users with these four questions to find the right price range:

1. At what price would this app feel too cheap to trust?
2. At what price would this app feel like a good deal?
3. At what price would this app feel expensive, but still worth it?
4. At what price would this app feel too expensive to buy?

The overlap of these ranges reveals your "acceptable price band."

**Common price ranges for Western markets**
- Monthly plan: $4.99–$12.99 (around $7.99 tends to perform well)
- Annual plan: Equivalent to roughly 10 months of the monthly price (~17% discount)
- Family plan: Around 1.5x the individual monthly price (covers multiple devices)

### Designing the Free Trial

Free trials are one of the most effective tools for improving subscription conversion rates.

```
Recommended trial lengths:
- 7 days: Simple utility apps
- 14 days: Habit-based apps (fitness, learning)
- 30 days: High-investment productivity apps
```

The most important thing in a free trial isn't the length — it's making sure users feel, by the end of the trial, that they can't imagine going without the app. That requires deliberate onboarding: deliver clear value on day 1, again on day 3, and again by day 7.

### Reducing Churn

Churn rate is one of the most important metrics in a subscription business.

**Detect early warning signals**
These behaviors often precede cancellation:

- Session frequency drops sharply (e.g., daily → weekly)
- Core feature usage stops
- Push notification engagement drops to zero

When you detect these signals, launch a re-engagement campaign: personalized push notifications, limited-time offers, or a proactive support outreach.

**Optimize the cancellation flow**
Making the cancel button hard to find can reduce short-term churn, but it damages trust and hurts you long-term. Instead:

- Ask why the user is canceling (use the feedback to improve)
- Offer a "pause" option as an alternative to canceling
- Before they cancel, show what they'd be giving up

## 3. Optimizing Ad Revenue

### Choosing the Right Ad Format

Here are the primary ad formats for mobile apps and how they compare.

**Banner ads**
Always-on ads displayed at the top or bottom of the screen.

- eCPM: Low ($0.50–$3.00 per thousand impressions)
- Impact on UX: Minimal
- Best for: Long-session apps (games during wait states, utilities)

**Interstitial ads (full-screen)**
Full-screen ads shown at natural breaks — between game levels, during app transitions.

- eCPM: Medium to high ($5.00–$20.00 per thousand impressions)
- Impact on UX: Moderate (timing is critical)
- Best for: Between game levels, content page transitions

**Rewarded video ads**
Ads that users choose to watch in exchange for in-app rewards (extra lives, coins, etc.).

- eCPM: High ($10.00–$50.00 per thousand impressions)
- Impact on UX: Low (user-initiated)
- Best for: Games, learning apps

### Timing Your Ad Displays

Maximizing ad revenue means finding display moments that feel natural and don't frustrate users.

**Guidelines for interstitials**
- Show only after natural breakpoints: game over, level complete
- Cap the number of interstitials per session (recommended: 3–5)
- Require at least 60 seconds between consecutive interstitials
- Never show one immediately at app launch

**Designing rewarded ads**
- Display when users want to continue but have run out of resources
- Make the reward value clear ("Watch an ad for +3 lives")
- Never force them — always provide a skip or dismiss option

### Basic AdMob Setup

Here's the general implementation flow for adding AdMob to your Rork app.

```
AdMob implementation steps:
1. Create a Google AdMob account and register your app
2. Obtain your ad unit IDs for each format you need
3. Configure the Google Mobile Ads SDK in your Rork project
4. Implement components for each ad format
5. Test with test ad IDs before going live
6. Switch to production IDs before your app store release
```

**Important: Always use test ad IDs during development.** Using production ad IDs in testing violates Google's policies and can get your account suspended.

## 4. Designing In-App Purchases

### Pricing Strategy: The Anchoring Effect

For consumable in-app purchases, a tiered pricing structure that uses the anchoring effect tends to perform well.

```
Example pricing tiers (game app):
- Starter pack: $0.99 (500 coins)
- Standard pack: $4.99 (2,500 coins) ← typically the most purchased
- Value pack: $9.99 (6,000 coins) ← best perceived value
- Premium pack: $24.99 (20,000 coins)
```

Showing the highest-priced option first makes the middle tiers feel like good value — that's anchoring at work.

### Combining with Subscriptions

When you combine subscriptions and in-app purchases, keep the relationship between them clear.

- **Subscription**: Ongoing access to features or services
- **In-app purchases**: Consumables (coins, extra attempts) or permanent content unlocks

Make sure these two don't overlap in confusing ways. Users should immediately understand what they're buying and what they already have access to.

## 5. The Revenue Improvement Cycle

### Key KPIs to Track

Regularly measure these KPIs to drive continuous improvement.

**Subscription metrics**
- MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)
- Free-to-paid conversion rate (after trial end)
- Churn rate (target: below 3% monthly)
- LTV (Customer Lifetime Value): average subscription duration × monthly price

**Ad revenue metrics**
- DAU (Daily Active Users)
- Ad impressions per session
- eCPM (earnings per 1,000 impressions)
- ARPDAU (average daily revenue per user)

**In-app purchase metrics**
- Paying user rate (paying users / total users)
- ARPPU (average revenue per paying user)
- Best-selling and underperforming items

### Improve with A/B Testing

Price, CTA copy, and offer timing all respond well to A/B testing.

```
Example A/B tests:

Test 1: Monthly subscription price
  A: $7.99/month
  B: $5.99/month (displayed as a limited-time price)
  Measure: conversion rate, LTV

Test 2: Subscription CTA copy
  A: "Upgrade to Premium"
  B: "Try Free for 7 Days"
  Measure: tap rate, conversion rate

Test 3: Rewarded ad offer timing
  A: Immediately after game over
  B: 3 seconds after game over
  Measure: view rate, user satisfaction
```

## Conclusion

Monetizing your app is just as much a design challenge as building the features themselves. The only sustainable path to growing revenue is delivering genuine value — making users feel, every time they pay, that it was absolutely worth it.

Rather than chasing numbers in the short term, aim for the combination of user delight and a business model that holds up over time. Measure carefully, improve incrementally, and let data guide your decisions.
