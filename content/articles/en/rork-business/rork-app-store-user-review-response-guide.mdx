## Why Review Management Can Make or Break Your App

After publishing your Rork app to the App Store or Google Play, one of the first real-world challenges you'll face is managing **user reviews**.

Star ratings are one of the most visible trust signals in any app store. When browsing competing apps, most users treat **4.0 stars or above** as a baseline for credibility. An app sitting at 3.5 or below — no matter how polished the feature set — will consistently lose downloads to higher-rated alternatives.

Beyond perception, ASO research consistently shows that **developers who respond to reviews tend to see higher engagement and better algorithmic treatment**. Thoughtful responses signal to both users and the store that someone is actively maintaining and improving the product.

In this guide, we'll cover everything you need to manage reviews systematically for apps built with Rork — from navigating App Store Connect and Google Play Console, to response templates for every situation, to implementing in-app Rating Requests that grow your review count organically.

## How to Review and Respond in App Store Connect

### Getting to Your Reviews

1. Sign in to [App Store Connect](https://appstoreconnect.apple.com)
2. Select your app from "My Apps"
3. Navigate to **Feedback → Ratings and Reviews** in the left sidebar
4. Use the filters (language, star rating, date) to focus on what needs attention

### Sending a Reply

Click the **Reply** button beneath any review to open the editor. Once submitted, your response appears publicly under the review as "Response from Developer," and the reviewer receives a notification via their Apple ID.

**Key rules to keep in mind:**

- Responses are **visible to everyone** — write as if addressing your entire audience
- Never include personally identifiable information, even if the user shared theirs
- Replies **cannot be edited** after submission (you can delete and rewrite, but the timestamp changes)
- Match the language of the review whenever possible

### Apple's Response Policy

Apple's guidelines prohibit responses that:

- Disparage other apps or competitors
- Include personal contact information or external links to outside forms
- Offer incentives in exchange for a rating change (e.g., "Reply and I'll send you a promo code")

## Managing Reviews in Google Play Console

### Accessing Your Reviews

1. Open [Google Play Console](https://play.google.com/console)
2. Select your app → **Ratings and Reviews → Reviews**
3. Filter by star rating, language, or date range

### Sending a Reply

Click **Reply** below any review, enter your response, and click **Submit Reply**. Unlike App Store Connect, Google Play **allows you to edit responses** after submission — a small but helpful difference when you spot a typo or want to update with new information.

### Using the Google Play Reviews API (Advanced)

Once your app accumulates a meaningful volume of reviews, manually checking each one becomes tedious. The [Google Play Developer API](https://developers.google.com/android-publisher) provides a Reviews endpoint to automate retrieval and even bulk responses.

```javascript
// Google Play Reviews API — Node.js example
const { google } = require('googleapis');

async function listUnansweredReviews(packageName) {
  const auth = new google.auth.GoogleAuth({
    keyFile: 'service-account.json',
    scopes: ['https://www.googleapis.com/auth/androidpublisher'],
  });

  const androidpublisher = google.androidpublisher({ version: 'v3', auth });

  const response = await androidpublisher.reviews.list({
    packageName,
    translationLanguage: 'en',
  });

  // Index 0 = user comment, index 1 = developer reply
  const unanswered = response.data.reviews?.filter(
    review => !review.comments?.[1]
  ) || [];

  console.log(`Unanswered reviews: ${unanswered.length}`);
  return unanswered;
}

// Example usage
listUnansweredReviews('com.yourapp.package').then(reviews => {
  reviews.forEach(r => {
    const comment = r.comments[0].userComment;
    console.log(`★${comment.starRating} — ${comment.text}`);
  });
});
// Expected output:
// ★2 — Login keeps failing
// ★1 — App crashes on startup
```

## Response Templates for Low Ratings (1–3 Stars)

Responding to negative reviews follows a reliable three-part structure: **acknowledge → investigate → commit to improvement**. Doing so publicly demonstrates to prospective users that you take feedback seriously — which can meaningfully offset the impact of the low rating itself.

### Template A: Bug or Technical Issue Report

```
Thank you for letting us know about this issue — we're sorry for the trouble.
We're actively looking into [the specific problem] and want to get it resolved as quickly as possible.
If you're able to share your device model and iOS/Android version via [support contact], it would help us track this down faster.
We appreciate your patience and hope to have an update out soon.
```

### Template B: Feature Request or Functionality Complaint

```
Thanks for the honest feedback — we genuinely appreciate it.
[The feature you mentioned] is something we've heard from other users as well, and it's on our radar for an upcoming release.
We're committed to making the app better with each update, so stay tuned — your input is helping shape what comes next.
```

### Template C: Usability or Confusion About How Something Works

```
Thanks for taking the time to share your experience.
[The feature] can be accessed by [brief explanation — one or two sentences]. We also have a short walkthrough in Settings → Help if you'd like a step-by-step guide.
That said, if it wasn't intuitive, that's feedback we take seriously. We're continuously refining the UI to make things easier to discover.
```

## Response Templates for High Ratings (4–5 Stars)

Responding to positive reviews is easy to skip — but it matters. A short, personal acknowledgment turns a passive reviewer into a loyal advocate, and the visible exchange adds social proof for anyone reading before downloading.

### Template D: Simple Thank-You

```
Thank you so much for the kind words — it really means a lot!
We're glad [App Name] has been useful for you. We have some exciting improvements in the pipeline, so stay tuned for future updates.
```

### Template E: Responding to a Specific Compliment

```
This honestly made our day — thank you!
[The aspect they mentioned] is something we put a lot of care into, so hearing it's working well for you is incredibly motivating.
We've got [upcoming feature or improvement] coming in a future update — hope you'll enjoy it too!
```

## Implementing Rating Request in Your Rork App

The most reliable way to grow your review count is to **ask users at the right moment, inside the app**. Apple's `SKStoreReviewRequest` (StoreKit) and Google's In-App Review API both allow you to surface a native rating prompt without redirecting to the store — and `expo-store-review` makes this straightforward in Expo-based Rork apps.

```bash
# Install the package
npx expo install expo-store-review
```

```typescript
// src/utils/ratingRequest.ts
import * as StoreReview from 'expo-store-review';
import AsyncStorage from '@react-native-async-storage/async-storage';

const KEYS = {
  APP_OPENS: 'app_opens_count',
  LAST_REQUESTED: 'rating_last_requested',
  NEVER_ASK: 'rating_never_ask',
};

export async function maybeRequestReview(): Promise<void> {
  // 1. Respect "never ask again" preference
  const neverAsk = await AsyncStorage.getItem(KEYS.NEVER_ASK);
  if (neverAsk === 'true') return;

  // 2. Increment launch count
  const rawCount = await AsyncStorage.getItem(KEYS.APP_OPENS);
  const count = parseInt(rawCount || '0', 10) + 1;
  await AsyncStorage.setItem(KEYS.APP_OPENS, String(count));

  // 3. Check if 30 days have passed since the last prompt
  const lastRequested = await AsyncStorage.getItem(KEYS.LAST_REQUESTED);
  if (lastRequested) {
    const daysSince = (Date.now() - parseInt(lastRequested, 10)) / (1000 * 60 * 60 * 24);
    if (daysSince < 30) return;
  }

  // 4. Show prompt on the 5th launch (or every 30 days afterward)
  if (count >= 5 && (await StoreReview.isAvailableAsync())) {
    await StoreReview.requestReview();
    await AsyncStorage.setItem(KEYS.LAST_REQUESTED, String(Date.now()));
  }
}

// Usage: call this from your main screen's useEffect
// useEffect(() => { maybeRequestReview(); }, []);

// Result (iOS): Native App Store rating dialog appears in-app
// Result (Android): Google Play In-App Review dialog appears in-app
```

**Best practices for Rating Request timing:**

- Show the prompt **after a positive moment** — task completed, goal achieved, content enjoyed
- Never show it immediately on launch, after an error, or during a critical user flow
- Apple limits the prompt to **3 times per year** per device; the system silently ignores additional calls
- Android has no hard frequency limit, but overuse leads to poor ratings and user annoyance

## Building a Sustainable Review Management Routine

For indie developers, review management is one of those tasks that's easy to put off until the backlog becomes overwhelming. A few simple habits prevent that:

**Weekly review block**: Reserve 15 minutes on a consistent day — say, Monday morning — to check and respond to all reviews from the previous week. Batching this task prevents it from becoming a daily interruption.

**Turn on notifications**: App Store Connect lets you receive email alerts for new reviews (App → Notifications). Google Play Console has similar notification settings. Enabling these ensures you don't miss a critical bug report buried in a 1-star review.

**Log patterns in a spreadsheet**: A simple tracker with columns for date, rating, core complaint, and response status helps you spot trends. If "login not working" shows up three times in one week, that's a signal to investigate — not just respond.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Mistake 1: Responding defensively**

Receiving a 1-star review that says "this app is garbage" is frustrating — but a defensive or dismissive public reply does far more damage than the review itself. Always respond with the goal of solving a problem, not winning an argument. Potential users are watching.

**Mistake 2: Responding too late**

A reply that comes six weeks after the review was posted rarely changes anything — the user has likely moved on. Aim to respond within **three to seven days** while the experience is still fresh. Faster responses also increase the chance the user updates their rating.

**Mistake 3: Copy-pasting identical responses**

Templates are a useful starting point, but sending word-for-word identical replies to every review reads as robotic. At minimum, reference something specific the user mentioned — the feature name, the device, the workflow they described. That personal touch makes a meaningful difference.

## FAQ

**Q. Will replying to a low-rated review actually change the star rating?**

A. Not automatically. Your response doesn't alter the rating — only the user can do that. However, a thoughtful reply acknowledging the issue and mentioning an update often prompts users to voluntarily raise their rating. You can mention that the problem has been fixed in a recent update and invite them to revisit the experience, but avoid any language that pressures or incentivizes a change — that violates both Apple's and Google's guidelines.

**Q. What can I do about obviously fake or spam reviews?**

A. Both App Store Connect and Google Play Console have a **Report** feature for reviews that violate platform policies. If Apple or Google agrees, the review can be removed — though there's no guarantee. In the meantime, respond calmly and factually. A measured, professional reply to an unfair review actually builds credibility with readers who can tell the review is exaggerated or bad-faith.

**Q. What's the best moment to show the Rating Request dialog?**

A. Immediately after a user accomplishes something meaningful in your app. For a task manager, that's completing a task. For a fitness app, it's finishing a workout. For a language learning app, it's completing a lesson. Avoid triggering the prompt at launch, after errors, or during payment flows. The user's emotional state at the moment of the prompt has a significant effect on the rating they leave.
