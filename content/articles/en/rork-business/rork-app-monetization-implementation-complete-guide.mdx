Once a Rork-built app is live and has produced its first hundred dollars, the real monetization design begins. The interesting finding — after ten years of shipping indie apps, and more recently using Rork to build new ones — is that **which monetization model you pick, and how you combine it, matters more than the app quality itself for revenue outcomes.** Two apps of similar quality routinely differ by 5× in revenue based on monetization choice and implementation.

This guide walks through picking a primary monetization model and implementing it well: AdMob, subscriptions, in-app purchase, and Stripe integration. What to use, how to combine, and how to operate each.

## Pick a Primary Axis Based on App Type

The single biggest monetization mistake indie developers make is trying to balance every model equally. Apps that lean clearly on one primary revenue axis outperform apps that hedge.

Four app archetypes, with their natural primary axis:

**Type A: Daily micro-utility.**
Budgeting, tasks, health logs. Short sessions, high frequency. **AdMob banner + rewarded video** is the natural base. Light IAP like "remove ads" works; heavy sub or IAP does not fit.

**Type B: Immersive content app.**
Meditation, language learning, fitness, reading. Long sessions, continuous experience. **Subscription** is the natural base. Ads break the experience; use minimal rewarded video if anything.

**Type C: Creator / editing tool.**
Photo editors, note-takers, illustration tools. Users self-identify as power users. **IAP (feature unlock) + subscription** is the strongest pairing. Ads actively drive away your most valuable users; avoid.

**Type D: Games.**
Genre-dependent. Generally **IAP (consumables) + rewarded video + banners.** Subscriptions only fit specific genres like "unlimited play passes."

Most Rork-built apps fall into A, B, or C. Naming your archetype up front tells you what to cut.

## AdMob: Implementation and Placement

For an ad-driven app, the implementation details that matter are placement rules, not revenue math.

**Banners** work best anchored to the bottom — except when your primary actions live at the bottom (tab bars, primary buttons). In that case, move the banner to the top or restrict it to specific screens where it doesn't compete.

```typescript
// React Native AdMob banner essentials
import { BannerAd, BannerAdSize, TestIds } from "react-native-google-mobile-ads";

const bannerUnitId = __DEV__
  ? TestIds.BANNER
  : "ca-app-pub-XXXXXXXXXXXXXXXX/YYYYYYYYYY";

export function AdBanner() {
  return (
    <BannerAd
      unitId={bannerUnitId}
      size={BannerAdSize.ANCHORED_ADAPTIVE_BANNER}
      requestOptions={{ requestNonPersonalizedAdsOnly: false }}
    />
  );
}
```

**Interstitials** are the most experience-destroying ad unit. My rule: **at most one per session, immediately after a completed primary action.** Never on app launch. Never on every screen transition.

**Rewarded video** is self-selected by users, so it doesn't damage retention. Best uses: "unlock this premium action once" or "unlock this extra content."

The biggest lever for AdMob revenue isn't which unit you use — it's your app's retention. High DAU with mediocre ad configuration outearns low DAU with a perfectly optimized stack. Fix retention first, monetize second.

## Subscriptions via RevenueCat

For native subscriptions, **RevenueCat** is the modern standard for indie developers. It wraps iOS and Android store purchases, webhooks, restores, and receipt validation into one API. Rolling your own is a one-way ticket to days of wasted debug time.

Core integration:

```typescript
import Purchases from "react-native-purchases";

await Purchases.configure({ apiKey: process.env.REVENUECAT_API_KEY });
await Purchases.logIn(userId);

const offerings = await Purchases.getOfferings();
const monthly = offerings.current?.availablePackages[0];

const { customerInfo } = await Purchases.purchasePackage(monthly!);
const isSubscribed = customerInfo.entitlements.active["pro"] !== undefined;
```

The critical subscription design decision is **trial length.** Three to seven days is standard, but the right answer depends on the app:

- Habit-formation apps (meditation, fitness): 7–14 days
- High-frequency utilities: 3 days is enough

Always ship a trial-expiration notification. The single most common one-star-review complaint is "I forgot it was a trial and got charged." Telling users transparently "your trial ends tomorrow — $X charged Monday" preserves long-term trust at the small cost of some conversions.

## Subscription UX That Lowers Churn

Maximizing subscription revenue is almost entirely about lowering churn. Three concrete mechanisms worth shipping:

**1. Engineer a first-day win during the trial.**
Users who experience a concrete value moment on day one of the trial are dramatically more likely to convert. Design the onboarding to deliver *one* unmistakable "I'm keeping this" moment.

**2. Intercept cancellations with a downgrade option.**
Before the user reaches the cancel button, offer a half-price reduced-feature plan. Keeping partial revenue beats losing it outright. LTV improvements are surprisingly large.

**3. Win-back campaigns 30 days after churn.**
Email churned users 30 days out with a "30 days free, come back and see what's new" offer. Conversion is 5–10%; the cost is essentially zero.

```typescript
async function handleCancelAttempt() {
  const currentPlan = await getCurrentPlan();
  if (currentPlan === "pro") {
    const accepted = await showDowngradeDialog({
      message: "Before you cancel Pro, would you like to switch to Lite (half price)?",
      options: ["Switch to Lite", "Cancel anyway"],
    });
    if (accepted) {
      await changePlan("lite");
      return;
    }
  }
  await openCancellationFlow();
}
```

## IAP: Tiered Pricing That Actually Nudges

One-time IAPs fit feature unlocks and content packs. The psychological advantage over subscriptions: "pay once, own forever" is easier for many users to say yes to.

My three-tier IAP structure:

- **Entry trial unlock**: $0.99–$2.99. A low-friction first purchase.
- **Standard premium**: $4.99–$9.99. Core feature unlock.
- **Complete bundle**: $14.99–$19.99. Everything unlocked permanently.

This three-tier anchoring works better than offering only the complete bundle. Most users start with the entry price, then upgrade once they trust the product.

Mixing IAP and subscription in the same app needs care — users get confused if they overlap. The cleanest split: **subscription covers ongoing updates; one-time IAP covers current feature set.** Frame it so a user can choose rationally without feeling they're being asked to pay twice.

## Stripe Integration: When Native Billing Isn't Enough

Stripe covers three patterns that native store billing can't handle well:

**1. Web-parity apps.**
If you have both a web and mobile version of the same subscription, let Stripe handle web and RevenueCat handle native. RevenueCat can track external purchases, so a user is "subscribed" regardless of which side they paid on.

**2. Marketplace-shape features.**
If users within the app generate content they can sell (PDFs, videos, images), Stripe Connect is the right tool. Apple/Google aren't designed to act as payment processors between your users.

**3. Direct B2B billing.**
Apple and Google subscriptions are terrible for B2B. When a business customer says "send us an invoice," use Stripe directly. You save the 15–30% platform fee, they get the invoice format they expect, everyone is happy.

```typescript
// Stripe Checkout session on Cloudflare Workers
export async function createCheckoutSession(userEmail: string, env: Env) {
  const stripe = new Stripe(env.STRIPE_SECRET_KEY);
  return stripe.checkout.sessions.create({
    mode: "subscription",
    customer_email: userEmail,
    line_items: [{ price: env.STRIPE_PRICE_ID_PRO, quantity: 1 }],
    success_url: `${env.APP_URL}/billing/thanks`,
    cancel_url: `${env.APP_URL}/billing/cancel`,
    metadata: { source: "rork-app-web" },
  });
}
```

For a web+native stack, wire Stripe webhooks to update RevenueCat entitlements (custom entitlements work for this). That way the mobile app doesn't need to know *where* the user paid — only that they have access.

## Timing: Where You Show the Ask Matters Enormously

The monetization prompt itself is a design surface. The wrong timing murders conversions even with perfect pricing.

Three moments that convert well:

- **Right after a first success.** Just completed the app's headline action? That's the moment to mention Pro.
- **At the free-tier ceiling.** The user wanted *more* of what they just did. Offering "go unlimited" at that exact moment is uniquely persuasive.
- **After 3+ consecutive days of use.** The user has voted with their return visits. A trial offer here lands as an invitation, not a demand.

The moment *not* to show the ask: the very first launch. Forcing a trial prompt before the user has seen any value is the fastest way to generate uninstall.

## The Five Metrics Actually Worth Tracking

Indie operators drown in metrics. Focus on five:

| Metric | Target | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| DAU / MAU (stickiness) | ≥ 20% | Top |
| Day 1 retention | ≥ 30% | High |
| Day 7 retention | ≥ 15% | High |
| ARPU | Category-dependent | Mid |
| Monthly subscription churn | ≤ 10% | Mid |

Fix retention before you fix monetization. Retention improvements compound into every downstream metric. Stacking monetization on top of a leaky retention bucket just accelerates the drain.

## The Tax and Accounting Side You Can't Ignore

Once indie revenue becomes real, the tax side stops being optional. Some universal guidance:

- Record AdMob, Apple, Google, and Stripe payouts separately, in their payout currency.
- Track any withholding at source (AdMob in particular) to avoid double taxation.
- File the appropriate small-business structure early; most tax regimes reward filing well before you need to.

Monthly bookkeeping beats a frantic year-end session every single time. Spend ten minutes at month-end to copy last month's payouts into whatever accounting software you use. Perfect is the enemy of sustainable — the goal is a routine you can actually keep up.

## Realistic Trajectory Lines

A realistic revenue curve for a Rork-app-focused indie developer:

- **3 months**: First app at $50–$100/month. Retention groundwork established.
- **6 months**: Second app shipped. $200–$500/month across both. Iteration velocity improves.
- **12 months**: 3–4 apps. $1,000–$3,000/month. Balancing maintenance and new builds.

Individual results vary wildly based on category and market timing. The real point is the pattern — **you build the muscle by shipping, and revenue follows muscle.**

With Rork in your toolbox, the constraint on indie success shifts from "can I build it" to "can I finish it and keep iterating." The difference between your first app and your fifth app is enormous. Stay in the loop, stack small releases, and the monetization picture fills itself in over the year.
