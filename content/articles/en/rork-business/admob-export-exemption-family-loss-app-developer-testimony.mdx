On April 16, 2026, after hanging up a call from the Toyama Tax Office, I was in an ambulance. My chest was racing, my stomach and throat were burning, and at the hospital I was given a sedative to take as needed and an intramuscular injection. Tomorrow — April 20, 2026 — I will walk into a lawyer's office for the first time.

Since 2014, I have built apps alone. Wallpaper apps, calming apps, law-of-attraction apps. With AdMob as the main revenue source, my peak months brought in more than ¥1.5 million (roughly USD $10,000). I remember being purely excited that my work was reaching phones all over the world, and that money was flowing back from that simple fact.

Today I am bankrupt, divorced, living in Toyama apart from my four children.

This article is written for **indie app developers who earn from overseas ad platforms — AdMob, Unity Ads, AppLovin, and similar**. Before any technical solution, I want to lay out the facts honestly, so that someone inside the same structure does not repeat my mistakes.

> Note: Japanese tax law specifics are used throughout, but the underlying pattern — cross-border ad revenue, export-exemption treatment, and statutes of limitations on refunds — has analogs in many countries. Please substitute your own jurisdiction's rules where relevant.

## AdMob Revenue Is an "Export Transaction" Under Japanese Consumption Tax

The most basic fact first. For Japan-resident sole proprietors, AdMob revenue paid by a non-resident counterparty (such as Google Ireland Ltd. or Google LLC) qualifies as **export-exempt** under Article 7 of the Consumption Tax Act. It is exempt from output consumption tax while still being counted as sales for threshold purposes.

This means that an indie developer earning primarily from AdMob has a fundamentally different consumption-tax structure from one earning domestic business income. In particular, most AdMob-first developers end up in a **refund position** every year: consumption tax paid on their expenses (servers, tools, hardware, user-acquisition spend) is refundable to the extent it corresponds to export-exempt sales.

Handled correctly, this is lawful and routine. Handled poorly — or unilaterally reversed years later — it becomes, in my case, a ¥5M+ assessment plus the silent loss of refunds I was owed.

## What Happened to Me

From 2014 onward, I lived in Miyazaki Prefecture and filed consumption tax under the Miyazaki Tax Office's jurisdiction. I reported overseas ad revenue as export-exempt and received refunds, sometimes substantial, every year. At no point did the tax office raise a specific objection to that treatment at the time of filing.

In 2022, I moved to Toyama. Around that time, I received a phone call from Miyazaki:

> "We are transferring your overpaid consumption-tax matter to the Uozu Tax Office under the Toyama bureau at your current address."

I read that as a routine, good-faith handover — "the refund process will continue under the new office." What actually happened is this:

**Right around the point when refund claims under Article 74 of Japan's General Tax Act (five-year statute of limitations) would expire, the Uozu and Toyama tax offices initiated a full audit.** My prior export-exempt treatment was "not recognized." The refunds I was owed were declared time-barred. The assessment against me stacked up to more than ¥5 million.

I was left with no refunds, a large liability, and a tax office calling me at all hours.

## 2025: I Lost Everything

After the 2024 assessment notice, everything downstream collapsed. Consolidation-loan applications kept getting rejected — the contradiction between the earlier non-taxable treatment in Miyazaki and the reversed position in Toyama apparently sat on my credit profile like an open wound. Interest on existing debt kept compounding. The tax office kept demanding more.

In 2025 I filed for personal bankruptcy. Going bankrupt while my apps were still live and earning felt, to me as an indie developer, like having my own creations disowned. The same year, my marriage ended. I now live apart from my four children.

On quiet nights, I sometimes check dashboards for apps I built years ago and watch them still running on phones around the world. The apps that once felt like proof I could support a family ended up unable to.

## Three Structural Traps Specific to Indie App Developers

Looking back, I believe three patterns push indie app developers into this kind of outcome. I write them here so other developers can catch them earlier than I did.

### 1. Revenue comes from overseas, and the scale is larger than it looks

AdMob income in Japanese yen can feel modest month to month, but for consumption-tax purposes, it crosses the **¥10M-per-year threshold** (the legal line beyond which you become a taxable enterprise) far more easily than most developers expect. This is especially true when you ship multiple apps quickly — a common pattern with tools like Rork. The combined revenue across all apps is easy to lose sight of, and many developers discover they were already over the line several years ago.

The countermeasure: **from day one of your first app, keep all app revenue in a single automated spreadsheet**, month by month, app by app, platform by platform. Every new Rork app gets its revenue source wired into the same sheet.

### 2. Local tax accountants often do not know AdMob's structure

I will say this plainly: in many regional tax-accountant offices in Japan, you will not find someone who can confidently explain the consumption-tax treatment of AdMob revenue. Export-exemption determination, the legal definition of "non-resident," the place-of-supply rules — these are consumption-tax-specific areas that a corporate-focused accountant may rarely touch.

If you have a retained accountant, ask in your first meeting: "Do you treat AdMob revenue as export-exempt?" If the answer is vague, seek a second opinion. I wish I had done this earlier.

### 3. Misunderstanding what a tax-office "handover" actually means

Moving prefectures and having your jurisdiction change is routine for sole proprietors. But within that handover, it is possible for the incoming office to **wait out your refund-claim period rather than continue the refund process**. I did not know this was a possibility in 2022.

My current rule: **the moment you receive any handover notice, run it past a tax accountant or lawyer in writing.** Clarify what is being transferred, what action is expected from you, and on what timeline. Just that small step could have saved me from catastrophe.

## What to Set Up Now, With AI

If you are building with Rork and want to prepare defensively, three things are enough as a minimum:

**Centralize revenue sources.** Auto-aggregate revenue from every Rork app into a single Google Sheet — AdMob, Unity Ads, IAP, all of it — by month, app, and platform. Feed that sheet to Gemini or Claude and ask for estimated export-exempt portions on demand.

**Archive every document chronologically.** Any letter from a tax office or bank goes into a Google Drive folder named like `Tax_YYYY_MM_description`. Years later, that archive may be the evidence that saves you.

**Verify your position with AI, then with a professional.** A useful prompt:

> "I am a Japan-resident sole proprietor receiving approximately ¥X/month in ad revenue from AdMob (Google Ireland Ltd.). Explain the consumption-tax treatment under Article 7, Enforcement Order Article 17, and the place-of-supply rules for services provided to non-residents."

Do not treat the AI's answer as final. Use it as a starting point — "the AI explained it this way, what's your view?" — for a conversation with a human tax accountant. That opener raises the quality of the professional consultation dramatically.
