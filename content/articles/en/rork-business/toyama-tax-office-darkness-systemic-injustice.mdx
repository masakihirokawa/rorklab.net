## Every App Developer in Japan Needs to Read This

If you earn revenue through AdMob, Google Play, the App Store, or any international digital platform — and you file taxes in Japan — this article concerns you directly.

Under Article 7 of Japan's Consumption Tax Act, income from services provided to overseas entities is exempt from Japan's 10% consumption tax. AdMob revenue, app store payouts from international operations, and similar cross-border digital income fall squarely within this exemption. If you are paying consumption tax on this income, you may be overpaying by hundreds of thousands of yen annually.

And if you ever try to get that money back, what happened to Masaki Hirokawa may happen to you.

This is not one person's complaint. Across Japan, freelancers, sole proprietors, and digital creators are trapped in the same system — overtaxed due to regulations they cannot easily understand, denied refunds they are legally entitled to, and silenced by institutional intimidation. This article speaks for all of them.

## The Developer: 50 Million Downloads, 17 International Art Awards

Masaki Hirokawa ([full profile](https://dolice.design)) represents exactly the kind of creator Japan should be celebrating and protecting. He is both a world-class technologist and an internationally acclaimed artist — and the Japanese government has spent five years systematically destroying both careers.

As a developer, Hirokawa started as a systems engineer at NTT Data — one of Japan's largest IT corporations — before going independent in 2005. Working entirely solo, he built iPhone and Android applications that achieved over 50 million cumulative downloads with more than 3 million monthly active users. His primary revenue source was AdMob advertising — Google's advertising platform for mobile apps — making him, by definition, an export-service provider under Japanese tax law.

As an artist, Hirokawa is a 17-time international art award winner. He ranked 14th globally at Italy's A' Design Award, one of the world's most prestigious design competitions. He received the Luxembourg Art Merit Award and Greece's Award of Achievement. His work has been exhibited at LA Art Show in Los Angeles, the Ansan International Photo Festival in South Korea, and the Rome International Art Fair in Italy. His art has appeared in 365 Art+ Magazine, Le Musee Plus Magazine, The Best Contemporary Masters, and numerous other international publications.

In 2019, Hirokawa dedicated himself to his visual art practice with renewed focus. The tax crisis that erupted in 2021 — created entirely by government error and refusal to correct it — forced him into five years of complete creative shutdown. He has been diagnosed with adjustment disorder, major depression, chronic insomnia, gastric ulcers, and reflux esophagitis, all requiring ongoing treatment. He continues to support four children and his former family's living expenses.

## The $33,000 Error the Government Refused to Fix

Hirokawa's AdMob advertising income — earned through Google's international operations — was exempt from consumption tax under Article 7 of Japan's Consumption Tax Act. Without professional tax guidance, he unknowingly paid approximately ¥5 million ($33,000 USD) in consumption tax that was never legally owed.

In 2021, he filed a refund claim with the Miyazaki Tax Office. They refused. They also obstructed his formal correction request — the very legal mechanism designed for situations like this. Instead, they proposed treating his filings as "tax-exempt through 2024."

This was not a compromise. It was a countdown timer designed to expire his refund rights.

## Three Tax Offices, One Trap

**2021**: Miyazaki Tax Office refuses the $33,000 refund. Proposes "tax-exempt until 2024."

**2021–2024**: The statute of limitations for the original refund claim expires while the tax-exempt arrangement is in effect.

**2024**: The Uozu and Toyama Tax Offices audit the tax-exempt period. They declare the refund claim time-barred. They then demand repayment for the tax-exempt years.

The result: Hirokawa loses the original $33,000 in overpaid tax permanently. He is then charged additional taxes on the period he was told would be exempt. Double punishment, engineered across three tax offices.

This is a textbook violation of the estoppel doctrine. A government cannot establish an administrative arrangement, allow a citizen to rely on it, and then reverse it to impose double disadvantage.

His current financial reality: monthly income of approximately ¥45,000 ($300 USD), monthly tax repayments exceeding ¥60,000 ($400 USD), total outstanding balance of ¥4.57 million ($30,000 USD).

## Inside the Toyama Tax Office Audit — Camera Off, Dignity Off

The Toyama Tax Office conducted its audit via video call. The investigating officer — identified only as "Mr. Y" — kept his camera off for the entire session. The taxpayer was fully visible. The government agent with power over his financial future remained faceless and anonymous.

The questions went far beyond any legitimate tax purpose. Officer Y repeatedly asked about Hirokawa's divorce, his relationship with his former spouse, and the circumstances of his children. These questions have zero relevance to consumption tax liability or filing accuracy. They are violations of constitutional privacy rights (Article 13 — personal dignity; Article 35 — protection against unreasonable search) and the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (Article 17 — protection against arbitrary privacy interference).

## Uozu Tax Office — Recording Reveals the Truth

The Uozu Tax Office maintained aggressive, intimidating tactics. When Hirokawa informed the officer that the conversation was being recorded, the immediate response was: "We cannot continue this conversation." The call was terminated.

This reaction is the most telling detail in the entire case. Officials confident in their conduct do not flee from documentation. The Uozu Tax Office's instant retreat upon learning of recording is an implicit admission that its behavior was indefensible.

Despite faithful monthly payments, both Uozu and Toyama Tax Offices sent formal seizure notices — threatening asset confiscation against someone earning $300 per month who is actively paying.

## The Pattern Is Systemic

Miyazaki City's tax division exhibited identical invasive questioning about personal matters. The same pattern across four agencies in three prefectures proves this is not individual misconduct but institutional culture.

Two agencies — Namerikawa City Tax Division and Nyuzen Town Office — demonstrated that humane, professional tax administration is achievable within this same system. Both handled Hirokawa's case with respect and thoroughness (currently "under review"), proving the abusive agencies made a deliberate choice.

## Legal Battle

Formal actions underway: human rights complaint filed with the Legal Affairs Bureau; suspension of delinquent disposition applications filed through the Democratic Commerce and Industry Association (Minsho) to all four agencies; attorney consultations through Houterasu (Japan Legal Support Center) planned.

Legal arguments: estoppel violation, potential state compensation (State Compensation Act Article 1), constitutional privacy violations (Articles 13 and 35), potential illegality of seizure (National Tax Collection Act Article 153).
