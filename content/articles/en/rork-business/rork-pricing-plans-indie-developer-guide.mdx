## If the Pricing Page Isn't Telling You Enough

Rork's pricing page lists Free, Plus, Pro, and Max with columns for message counts, project visibility, SwiftUI support, and a handful of other toggles. On first read, it's genuinely hard to know which tier fits.

I climbed this ladder myself — Free, then Plus, Pro, and eventually Max. With that experience, the honest answer for most indie developers is: **Pro is the right tier most of the time, and Max is worth it only when specific conditions line up**.

This guide lays out what each plan actually gets you in indie practice, and — more usefully — what it doesn't. Quota math alone won't help you pick; the feature gates are where the real choices live.

## The Plans as of April 2026

**Free** — 30 messages/month, public projects only. An introduction, not a working plan. You'll use it to decide whether Rork's output style fits your taste.

**Plus ($20/mo)** — 500 messages/month, private projects, Companion app access. The hobbyist and side-project tier.

**Pro ($50/mo)** — 2,000 messages/month, App Store submission from inside Rork, priority support, parallel project work. The tier most people doing real indie shipping should live on.

**Max ($200/mo)** — 10,000 messages/month, native SwiftUI generation (Rork Max), visionOS, custom domain, team features. The tier for people producing commercial apps at volume.

Prices shift — Rork adjusts this stuff faster than most vendors. Always check [Rork's pricing page](https://rork.com/pricing) before committing.

## How Quickly You'll Actually Burn Messages

Message budgets matter more than anything else. Here are my real-world numbers:

**Small app, start to finish**: 50–80 messages for the initial prototype, then 50–100 more for iPad adaptation, dark mode, and polish. Roughly **100–180 messages per shipped small app**.

**Mid-complexity app**: account flows, push notifications, IAP — expect 300–500 messages plus another 50–100 for App Store iteration.

**Post-launch maintenance**: bug reports, OS version adaptation, small features — plan on 50–150/month per active app.

Mapping that to tiers:

- One small app a month → Plus covers it
- One to two apps a month, multiple parallel projects → Pro is needed
- Three or more a month, or a large flagship app → Max starts making sense

Free at 30 messages can't finish one small app. Treat it as a test drive, not a development plan.

## Why Private Projects Matter More Than You'd Think

The overlooked detail is this: **on Free, all your projects are public**. Anyone on Rork can read them.

Most indie projects don't want that. Ideas in motion, proprietary logic, commercial apps in development — you don't want competitors browsing the code while you build. The moment this hits you, you're on Plus anyway. Easier to start there than to move mid-project with the nagging worry that something already leaked.

This feature alone — independent of the extra messages — is reason enough to skip Free for anything you care about.

## When Max Is Actually Worth $200/month

Max's marquee feature is Rork Max — native SwiftUI generation. It's real, it's good, and it's also expensive. I'd argue indie developers should only move to Max when *all* of these are true:

- You specifically need iOS-native quality (SwiftUI over React Native isn't optional)
- You ship more than two apps a month (2,000 messages genuinely isn't enough)
- You target Vision Pro (visionOS support lives here)
- You have commercial revenue that can cover $200/month

If any of those is "no," Pro will serve you better. I ran on Pro for the first year and only moved to Max when personal-app revenue reliably crossed $1,000/month. Structurally, "Pro until revenue exists, then Max" has been a healthier cadence than upgrading on hope.

## Annual vs. Monthly

Rork discounts annual plans ~20% (Pro at $480/year, Max at $1,920/year).

My recommendation: **monthly for the first three months, annual once you've confirmed the tool fits**. The AI app-builder space is moving fast — Lovable, Bolt, FlutterFlow, and whatever ships next may shift the calculus before your renewal. As an indie, keeping optionality is often worth 20%.

## What Each Plan *Can't* Do

Comparing features instead of prices is usually how you avoid mistakes. Each plan's limits:

- **Free**: no private projects, no App Store submission, no Companion, 30-message ceiling
- **Plus**: no App Store submission from inside Rork (you'll export and submit from Xcode yourself), no SwiftUI/Rork Max, no Vision Pro
- **Pro**: no Rork Max (SwiftUI native), no visionOS, no custom domain
- **Max**: no caps that matter for most indie workloads (overages purchasable)

The most common confusion: **Pro does not include native SwiftUI generation**. Rork Max is Max-only. People sign up for Pro expecting SwiftUI output and hit surprise.

## A Sensible Indie Ladder

Based on the path that actually worked for me:

**Step 1 — Free, 1–2 weeks.** Build a small Todo app. Does the output style match how you'd write code? Decide on fit.

**Step 2 — Plus, about a month.** Build the real minimum version of the app you actually want to ship, privately.

**Step 3 — Pro, 1–2 months.** Use in-Rork App Store submission to actually get the app on the store. This is where most indie dev lives long-term.

**Step 4 — Max, from month 3+ once justified.** When one or more personal apps pulls in $500–$1,000+/month reliably, Max's extra budget starts to pay for itself.

You don't need to jump to Max. Indie shipping is a long game; match the tier to your current scale and upgrade when reality demands it.

## Ship One, Learn Which Tier You Need

The correct plan depends on how you develop and where your revenue is. Use the guidance above as signal, not a rule.

More than the plan decision: **ship one app end-to-end**. Going through a full build → submission → live-on-store loop teaches you which limits you care about and which you don't, and that's what should drive your next tier choice.
