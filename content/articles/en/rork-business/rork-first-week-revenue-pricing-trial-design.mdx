Shipping a Rork-built app to the App Store and Google Play is genuinely fast now. My most recent release went from blank page to App Review submission in two weeks. But first-week revenue does not scale with build speed. I still remember releases where 100 downloads in week one converted to a single paying user.

The cause was the same every time: I assumed I could "release first, optimize after." The opposite is closer to the truth. First-week revenue is mostly decided by what is in place on launch morning. Post-launch tweaks rarely recover the gap.

This article is the operating checklist I now run on Rork-built launches — pricing, free trial, store funnel, and first-run UX — to keep first-week revenue on a healthy trajectory.

## Why "fix it after launch" arrives too late

The App Store and Google Play place heavy weight on launch-week signals. DAU, retention, conversion rate, and reviews in the first seven days seem to feed the algorithms that decide your visibility in featured placements and category rankings. The exact mechanics are not documented, but after observing 100+ launches I am highly confident the relationship is real.

Apps that perform well in week one ride a positive loop where organic traffic in week two pushes the numbers further, which compounds again. Apps that stumble in week one almost never enter that loop, no matter how much I rework them later. I have a handful of apps in my catalog that received complete pricing/screenshot/copy overhauls three months post-launch — none recovered to the level of apps that launched well.

So "first-week revenue" is not really about seven days of money — it is about the next twelve months of trajectory. That is why launch-morning prep matters so much.

## Design point 1: Test pricing, do not pick it

Most Rork-built apps face a choice early on: subscription, one-time purchase, or both. The most common mistake I see beginners make is "looked at competitors and copied their price." Competitor pricing is the result of a year or more of optimization on a more mature product. Carry it over to a new app and users perceive the price as too high for the feature depth.

For first launches I always prepare three tiers:

- **Entry tier**: under half of competitor pricing — roughly $1.99–2.99/month
- **Standard tier**: matching competitor pricing — roughly $3.99–5.99/month
- **Premium tier**: above competitor pricing — roughly $7.99–12.99/month

Two weeks before launch I distribute these across regions/countries — using App Store Connect pricing schedules on iOS and Play Console subscription A/B testing on Android. Assuming the same price elasticity in Japan and North America is risky, so the regional split is in from day one.

After seven days of data, I unify on whichever tier maximized "conversion rate × ARPU." In my experience, the answer often lands between the entry and standard tiers — somewhere around $2.99/month. Launching straight at the standard tier would have hidden the elasticity ceiling I only saw because the entry tier was running.

## Design point 2: Design free trials around "experiences," not days

Whether to run a 7-day or 3-day trial is the wrong axis. The real question is: **does the user experience the core value at least twice during the trial?**

The criteria I use:

- **What is the core value of this app, in one sentence?**
- **How many minutes does it take to experience that value once?**
- **What is the natural interval before the user wants to feel that value again?**

For a meditation app, the core value might be "feeling lighter after a five-minute session," with a natural interval of 24–48 hours. A 3-day trial only delivers one or two sessions, so conversion will not move. Either extend to seven days, or build a launch-day flow that delivers two experiences (morning and evening notifications).

For a once-a-month utility (think tax-filing helpers), no trial length will deliver a second experience. There the answer is to switch to one-time purchase, or to annual-only subscription that frames the value as "worth it once a year."

## Design point 3: Design the store funnel for the first three seconds

Users spend an average of three to five seconds on a single app's store page. My own intuition matches: the first screenshot, the icon, and the title decide whether they tap.

The launch-morning checks I always run:

**First screenshot.** Communicate the core value in one image. Limit text overlay to about seven characters in English (or fewer in Japanese), avoid emoji, and prioritize legibility. The test I use: "Could the person sitting next to me on a train get the meaning from a peripheral glance?"

**Icon.** When placed alongside competitors on a home screen, does it stand out or blend in? Rork-generated icons can come out feeling templated, so a week before launch I lay mine alongside competitor icons in a home-screen mockup.

**Title.** App name plus a short feature descriptor (under 30 characters). Search ranking depends heavily on this, so include one or two words your target user actually types. "Zen Timer — One-Minute Mindful Reset" packs both function and time commitment, lifting click-through.

**Screenshots 2 through 5.** Do not enumerate features. Show the four moments a user will live through with the app, in chronological order. Settings screens have no place here.

## Design point 4: Design the first 30 seconds of the app

Even with the funnel doing its job, about 40% of users drop off inside the first 30 seconds. This is true across mobile in general, not specific to Rork apps, but a weak first-run UX guarantees first-week revenue stays flat.

My standard first-run flow:

1. **In the first 5 seconds, hint at the core value.** No login screen or signup form first. Guest mode or a "try it now" button at the top.
2. **In the next 10 seconds, deliver the core value once.** A 30-second sample meditation, a one-feature demo run for utilities. Communicate "what this app does" through action, not copy.
3. **In the next 15 seconds, present the path to a second experience.** Notification permission, favorites, initial settings — in that order. Whether you earn the notification permission here decides next-day retention.

Push notification permission belongs immediately after the first value experience, not at the start. Asked first, refusal rates exceed 60%. Asked right after the value moment, acceptance crosses 70%. I have an app where moving the prompt alone lifted Day-1 retention from 18% to 32%.

## The launch-morning checklist

Finally, the actual checklist I run on launch morning. If anything below fails, I delay launch by a day.

- Three pricing tiers prepared and distributed across regions for A/B
- Trial flow surfaces the core value at least twice within the trial period
- First screenshot passes the "three-second peripheral test"
- Icon stands out next to competitor icons on a home screen mockup
- Core value experienced within 30 seconds of first launch
- Notification permission prompt placed right after the first value moment
- Funnel analytics (install → first run → value moment → conversion) instrumented in App Analytics or Firebase
- Review prompt fires only after the third value moment
- First three lines of the store description convey the core value
- Launch-day social posts and images prepared

This list is the union of every gap that hurt revenue when I shipped without it. Even one open item is enough to delay. A one-day launch slip costs little; a stumbled launch week locks in twelve months of trajectory.

Rork made shipping fast. Use the recovered time to spend one or two days on revenue prep before launch. Run through the checklist above on your next release — that is the highest-leverage place to start.
