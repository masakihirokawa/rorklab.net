Building an app with Rork is one thing. Getting it onto the App Store is another challenge entirely — and it's the step where many first-time developers stall. The build works fine, the app looks great, and then the rejection email arrives with vague language about guideline violations.

This guide walks through every step of the App Store submission process for Rork-built apps, from setting up your Apple Developer account to handling the common rejection scenarios. If you follow this through, you'll know exactly what to do at each stage.

## Before You Submit: What to Have Ready

The rejection rate drops significantly when submissions are properly prepared. Here's what to have in order before you touch the submit button.

### Apple Developer Account

Publishing to the App Store requires an Apple Developer Program membership ($99/year). If you haven't enrolled yet, start at [developer.apple.com](https://developer.apple.com). After joining, confirm:

- **Bundle ID registered**: Use the format `com.yourname.appname` — this must match what's in your Xcode project
- **Signing configured**: Enable automatic signing in Xcode's Signing & Capabilities tab; it's the simplest approach for most apps
- **App created in App Store Connect**: Create the app record in App Store Connect before you try to upload a build

### App Store Listing Content

Prepare all of this before sitting down to fill out the submission form:

- App name (max 30 characters)
- Subtitle (max 30 characters)
- Description (max 4,000 characters — lead with the value proposition, not what the app "is")
- Keywords (max 100 characters, comma-separated)
- Category (primary and optional secondary)
- Age rating
- Privacy policy URL (required if you collect any user data)

**Screenshots (required):**

At minimum, you need screenshots for:
- 6.9-inch display (iPhone 16 Pro Max): 1320 × 2868px
- 6.5-inch display (iPhone 14 Plus): 1242 × 2688px

Canva and Figma both have App Store screenshot templates that make this straightforward. You can also use Rork's own preview output as a starting point.

## Building and Uploading from Rork to Xcode

Rork exports generated code as an Xcode project. Here's how to get from Rork output to a build uploaded to App Store Connect:

```
1. In Rork, export the finished app code (downloads as a .zip)
2. Unzip and open the .xcodeproj or .xcworkspace in Xcode
3. In Signing & Capabilities, select your Apple Developer team
4. Confirm Bundle Identifier matches what you registered in App Store Connect
5. Set the scheme destination to "Any iOS Device (arm64)"
6. Go to Product → Archive
7. When Organizer opens, click "Distribute App" → "App Store Connect" → "Upload"
8. Wait for the build to appear in App Store Connect (usually 5–30 minutes)
```

Once the build appears under the Builds section of your app in App Store Connect, you're ready to submit.

## The Submission Process

With a build ready and listing content prepared:

```
1. In App Store Connect, open your app and create a new version (+Version)
2. Fill in all app information fields (name, description, screenshots, etc.)
3. Under "Build," select the uploaded build
4. Fill in App Review Information (demo account if login is required, notes for reviewers)
5. Click "Submit for Review"
```

Review times are typically 24–48 hours. During peak periods (late December, WWDC week), expect 3–7 days. You'll receive an email when the status changes.

## Common Rejection Reasons — and How to Fix Them

First-time rejections are common. The key is treating them as feedback, not failure. Read the rejection notice carefully, fix what it describes, and resubmit.

### Rejection: Guideline 2.1 — App Completeness

The reviewer decided the app is too minimal or incomplete to warrant App Store listing.

**Fix**: Ensure every feature that's visible in the UI actually works. Remove placeholder content or UI elements that don't do anything. Even simple apps pass if they clearly serve a defined purpose and deliver on it.

### Rejection: Guideline 5.1.1 — Privacy Policy Missing or Inadequate

If your app collects any user data (login, location, contacts, analytics), a privacy policy URL is mandatory.

**Fix**: Create a basic privacy policy using a free generator like [PrivacyPolicygenerator.info](https://www.privacypolicygenerator.info), host it (GitHub Pages works), and add the URL in App Store Connect. The policy doesn't need to be elaborate for a simple app.

### Rejection: Guideline 4.0 — Design Issues

The reviewer found the UI inconsistent with Apple's Human Interface Guidelines.

**Fix**: Rork's generated code generally follows HIG, so this usually comes up when you've added heavy customization. Before submitting, test every screen on a real device and make sure nothing crashes. Reviewers manually test the app, so any crash is a guaranteed rejection.

### Rejection: Missing Demo Account for Login-Protected Apps

If your app requires a login and you haven't provided reviewer credentials, reviewers can't test the app.

**Fix**: Add demo credentials in the "App Review Information" section of App Store Connect before submitting. A simple test account with pre-populated data is ideal.

## After Approval: First Week Checklist

Once your app is live:

**Monitor crashes**: Check Xcode Organizer or App Store Connect's Crashes section in the first 24–48 hours. Public traffic will surface edge cases that testing missed.

**Respond to reviews**: Your first reviews carry outsized weight. Responding to negative feedback professionally signals to both users and Apple that the app is actively maintained.

**Plan the first update**: The App Store algorithm favors apps that update regularly. A small bug fix or minor improvement within the first few weeks keeps the listing active.

## Getting Through the Finish Line

The submission process has more steps than most developers expect the first time, but it's fully learnable. Each submission teaches you something — what to prepare earlier, what the reviewers actually look for, where the friction is.

The mental model that helps most: a rejection isn't a verdict on your app. It's a checklist item with a specific fix. Read the guideline reference, make the change, resubmit. Most first-time apps get through within two or three attempts.

Your first App Store listing is the hardest. Build the habit of shipping, and everything that follows gets easier.
