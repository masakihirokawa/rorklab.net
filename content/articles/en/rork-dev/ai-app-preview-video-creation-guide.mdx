App Store preview videos are one of the most powerful tools in your arsenal for app marketing—but creating them doesn't have to mean hiring a videographer or wrestling with complex screen recording software. If you're building apps with Rork, you already know that time and resources are precious. That's where AI comes in.

This guide shows you how to craft professional, engaging preview videos using AI-powered tools, from script generation to final composition. You'll bypass the traditional headaches of screen recording and video editing, and deliver content that actually converts.

## Why Preview Videos Matter for App Discovery

Before diving into the how, let's talk about the why. App Store preview videos appear in the first moments a potential user sees your app. They sit right next to your screenshots, above your description, and they have a measurable impact on conversion rates.

Research shows that apps with preview videos see a 10-20% lift in conversion rates compared to text and screenshots alone. Google Play explicitly recommends preview videos as a key ASO (App Store Optimization) element. For Rork developers, this means preview videos are not a nice-to-have—they're essential to your go-to-market strategy.

The traditional workflow, however, has been painful. You'd need to:
- Set up screen recording with proper resolution and framing
- Perform every action perfectly in one take (or be prepared to edit dozens of clips)
- Use expensive editing software like Final Cut Pro or Adobe Premiere
- Spend hours color grading, adding transitions, and syncing music
- Export multiple formats for different platforms

AI tools now let you skip most of that friction. Instead of recording your actual screen, you can generate or synthesize video content, use AI to write compelling scripts, and automate the assembly process.

## The AI-Powered Preview Video Workflow

The most efficient approach involves three layers:

1. **Script Generation** — AI writes the narrative that explains your app's key features
2. **Visual Composition** — AI or template-based tools create the video frames, text overlays, and transitions
3. **Final Assembly** — Scripts, visuals, and audio are stitched together with minimal manual effort

Let's walk through each.

### Step 1: Generate Your Preview Script with AI

Your preview video script should be 30–60 seconds long (about 75–150 words). It needs to communicate:
- What problem your app solves (4–5 seconds)
- Your key differentiator (3–4 seconds)
- One or two core features in action (8–10 seconds each)
- A clear call-to-action (3–4 seconds)

Use an AI language model to draft this. Here's a prompt template:

```
Write a 45-second App Store preview script for [App Name].
The app [one-sentence description].
Key features: [list 2-3 features].
Target audience: [describe briefly].
Tone: [casual / professional / playful].
Include one hook at the start to grab attention in the first 2 seconds.
```

For example, if you're building a habit tracking app with Rork:

```
Write a 45-second App Store preview script for "HabitFlow," a habit-tracking app
that uses AI to adapt to your lifestyle.
Key features: Smart habit suggestions, streak tracking, community challenges.
Target audience: Young professionals (25-35) who want to build better habits.
Tone: Motivational and approachable.
Include a hook in the first 2 seconds that feels relatable.
```

The AI will give you something like:

> *"Ever started a habit you couldn't stick with? HabitFlow changes that. Our AI learns your lifestyle and suggests habits that actually fit. Track your progress with streaks, challenge friends, and watch yourself transform. No judgment, no guilt—just real results. Download HabitFlow today and build habits that last."*

Refine this once or twice, then move to visual composition.

### Step 2: Create Visuals with AI and Templates

You have two main options here:

**Option A: AI Video Generation (Text-to-Video)**

Tools like Runway, Synthesia, or Pika let you generate video clips from text descriptions. You provide a detailed description of each scene, and the tool creates realistic footage.

For app preview videos, this works well for:
- Background scenes (an office, a gym, a coffee shop where the habit happens)
- Transition sequences
- Animation of abstract concepts (data flowing, connections forming)

**Example prompt for an AI video generator:**

```
Generate a 3-second clip showing a young woman in a coffee shop,
smiling at her phone, with soft morning sunlight coming through the window.
She looks happy and focused. Style: cinematic, warm lighting, modern aesthetic.
```

**Option B: Template-Based Composition (Recommended for Beginners)**

If full AI video generation feels overwhelming, use templates from platforms like:
- Animaker (purpose-built for app previews)
- Envato Elements (thousands of mobile UI mockup animations)
- Motion (by Stripe—excellent for SaaS/app visuals)

These let you plug in your app screenshots, add text overlays, and choose from pre-designed transitions. The result looks polished without requiring video editing skills.

### Step 3: Add Voiceover and Music

**AI Voiceover:**

Tools like ElevenLabs, Google Cloud Text-to-Speech, or Synthesia generate natural-sounding narration directly from your script. The quality is now genuinely professional—no more robotic voices.

Upload your script (the one you refined in Step 1), select a voice that matches your brand tone, and generate the audio file. Many tools let you emphasize certain words or adjust pacing.

**Music and Sound Design:**

Pick royalty-free music from:
- Epidemic Sound
- Artlist
- YouTube Audio Library
- Fiver (hire a composer for custom scores)

Choose something that:
- Matches your app's vibe (energetic for fitness apps, calm for meditation apps)
- Isn't too loud (your voiceover should be the star)
- Has a clear beat that syncs with visual transitions

Many music libraries let you find tracks by mood and duration—search for "motivational," "uplifting," or "corporate" depending on your brand.

## Putting It Together: The ffmpeg Workflow

Once you have your script, visuals, voiceover, and music, you'll likely have a collection of files:

- `preview_video.mp4` (your main visual composition)
- `voiceover.mp3` (AI-generated narration)
- `background_music.mp3` (licensed audio)
- `logo.png` (your app icon)

**ffmpeg** is the industry standard for stitching these together. If you're automating this for multiple app variants, ffmpeg is your friend.

Here's a practical example:

```bash
# Combine voiceover and background music with proper levels
ffmpeg -i voiceover.mp3 -i background_music.mp3 \
  -filter_complex "[0]volume=1.0[a1];[1]volume=0.3[a2];[a1][a2]amix=inputs=2:duration=longest[out]" \
  -map "[out]" -c:a aac -b:a 128k audio_mix.mp3

# Overlay the audio mix onto your video, adding the logo in the bottom-right
ffmpeg -i preview_video.mp4 -i audio_mix.mp3 -i logo.png \
  -filter_complex "[0][2]overlay=W-w-10:H-h-10[v];[v][1]concat=n=1:v=1:a=1[out]" \
  -map "[out]" -c:v libx264 -crf 18 -c:a aac \
  final_preview.mp4

# Export for different platforms (iOS square, Android vertical, etc.)
ffmpeg -i final_preview.mp4 -vf "scale=1080:1920" -c:v libx264 -preset fast \
  preview_vertical.mp4

ffmpeg -i final_preview.mp4 -vf "scale=1440:1440" -c:v libx264 -preset fast \
  preview_square.mp4
```

This approach lets you batch-process multiple videos with consistent quality and branding.

## Platform-Specific Optimizations

App Store (iOS) and Google Play have different requirements. Don't overlook these details—they affect how your video displays.

**Apple App Store:**
- Maximum 30 seconds (soft limit; up to 30 is best)
- Aspect ratio: 9:16 (portrait, full-screen)
- Resolution: 1080 × 1920 pixels minimum
- Format: MP4 or MOV
- No audio required (autoplay is muted; captions preferred)

**Google Play Store:**
- Maximum 30 seconds recommended
- Aspect ratio: 9:16 (portrait) or 16:9 (landscape)
- Resolution: 1440 × 2560 pixels for best quality
- Format: MP4 or WebM
- Audio is heard (so voiceover matters more)

For maximum reach, create both a **muted version (with bold text overlays and visual effects)** and a **spoken version (with voiceover)**. Use the muted version for iOS (where autoplay is silent) and the spoken version for Google Play.

Here's how to add captions to your muted version using ffmpeg:

```bash
# Create an SRT subtitle file (preview_captions.srt)
# 1
# 00:00:00,000 --> 00:00:03,000
# Ever started a habit you couldn't stick with?
#
# 2
# 00:00:03,500 --> 00:00:08,000
# HabitFlow changes that with AI-powered suggestions.

# Burn subtitles into the video
ffmpeg -i preview_silent.mp4 -vf "subtitles=preview_captions.srt" \
  -c:v libx264 -crf 18 preview_with_captions.mp4
```

## Measuring What Works

Once you've published your preview video, monitor these metrics:

1. **Conversion Rate Lift** — Compare your conversion rate before and after adding the video. Look for a 10-20% improvement.
2. **Video Play Rate** — How many people who see your app page click play on the preview? Aim for 40-60%.
3. **Viewer Retention** — Where do people stop watching? If most viewers drop off at the 10-second mark, your opening hook needs work.
4. **A/B Testing** — Try different scripts, visual styles, or voiceover styles. Use two videos in separate app store listings (if your store supports it) to compare.

Tools like Sensor Tower, App Annie, and your store analytics dashboard provide these insights.

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

- **Too Much Text.** Don't cram 100 words into a 30-second video. Text should highlight, not replace, your voiceover.
- **Mismatched Pacing.** If your voiceover is fast but your visuals are slow, viewers feel disconnected. Sync them tightly.
- **Ignoring Platform Specs.** A video that looks perfect on your phone might be letterboxed or squished on the store. Always test on actual devices.
- **Weak Opening.** You have 2 seconds to hook people. If your first shot is a logo or a fade-in, you've already lost half your audience.
- **Copyright Issues.** Use royalty-free music and ensure your AI-generated content doesn't inadvertently use copyrighted material. Check your tool's terms.

## FAQ

**Q: Is AI-generated voiceover good enough, or should I hire a voice actor?**

A: Modern AI voiceovers are genuinely excellent—often indistinguishable from human actors. That said, if your brand is premium or highly emotional, a human voice might add polish. For most tech and lifestyle apps, AI is more than sufficient. Start with AI, and upgrade to human only if metrics show it matters.

**Q: What if my app is complex and has lots of features?**

A: Don't try to show everything. A preview video is a teaser, not a tutorial. Pick your single most compelling feature or use case, and focus on that. Deep feature explanations belong in screenshots and in-app onboarding. Remember, the video's job is to make someone want to download—not to explain how everything works.

**Q: How often should I update my preview video?**

A: At minimum, when you release a major feature or redesign. Some developers update quarterly or semi-annually to keep content fresh. Monitor retention metrics—if they drop below 40%, it's time for a refresh.
