Thanks for stopping by Rork Lab again this week.

Between April 25 and May 1, the most-read pieces clustered around two themes: an honest accounting of what Rork is good at after sustained use, and the business design that turns Rork-built apps into actual revenue. Here are the five that drew the most attention.

## #1: What Did Rork Actually Change as an AI Mobile App Builder? A Six-Month Field Review

After six months of using Rork on real personal products, this article steps back to ask what's genuinely different about it compared to the broader "AI app builder" category. The framing is intentionally about workflow rather than feature checklists.

The takeaway readers found most useful: Rork should be evaluated less by "how fast it generates an app" and more by "how easy is it to keep developing the app six months later." Plenty of tools will generate something that runs once. Far fewer make the next year of maintenance comfortable.

[What Did Rork Actually Change as an AI Mobile App Builder? A Six-Month Field Review](/articles/rork-ai/rork-ai-mobile-app-builder-review-2026)

## #2: Implementing CallKit + PushKit in Rork — A Production Guide for VoIP Apps

A complete production guide to building a VoIP calling app with Rork. The article walks through Apple's CallKit and PushKit integrations alongside the equivalent native incoming-call UI on Android, combining Rork's code generation with hand-written hardening.

VoIP is notoriously hard to test — backgrounding bugs, missing ringtones, calls that won't appear on the lock screen. The piece documents the specific potholes from my own production deployment with reproduction steps, so you can recognize them before users do.

[Implementing CallKit + PushKit in Rork: A Production Guide to Native Call UI and VoIP Push for Voice & Video Apps](/articles/rork-dev/rork-callkit-pushkit-voip-implementation-guide)

## #3: A Prompt Engineering Blueprint for Building Mobile Apps with Rork

A premium guide to the mobile-specific prompt engineering that gets the best out of Rork. It covers how to weave React Native, Expo, iOS, and Android idiosyncrasies into your prompts, plus how to review and harden the resulting scaffolds for production.

The lesson I found most striking while writing this: starting short and iterating in small reactions consistently outperforms trying to write a "perfect prompt" in one go. That observation generalizes well beyond Rork, but the article keeps it grounded in concrete mobile examples.

[A Prompt Engineering Blueprint for Building Mobile Apps with Rork](/articles/rork-dev/rork-prompt-engineering-mobile-blueprint)

## #4: Building a Multi-App Portfolio Business with Rork — A 90-Day Blueprint

Drawn from twelve years of indie app development, this premium article makes the case that running 3–5 apps in parallel is a more realistic path for solo developers than betting everything on a single hit, and lays out the operational design for doing so.

It abstracts genre selection, update cadence, store-listing optimization, and cross-promotion into a template, drawn from my own running-multiple-apps logs. If you're aiming for sustainable revenue from indie apps, this is meant to give you a concrete six-to-twelve-month picture.

[Building a Multi-App Portfolio Business with Rork — A 90-Day Blueprint from 12 Years of Indie Development](/articles/rork-business/rork-indie-portfolio-business-blueprint-2026)

## #5: Rork App Revenue Model Showdown — Ads vs Subscriptions vs One-Time Purchase

A premium article that compares the three primary monetization models with real numbers rather than abstract reasoning. Sample data comes from my own running apps, organized by genre and user profile.

The section that seemed to land hardest with readers: "hybrid is the realistic answer for solo developers." Layering ads and subscriptions in the same app generates real friction, and the article tries to articulate where those friction points sit and how user psychology responds to them.

[Rork App Revenue Model Showdown — Ads vs. Subscriptions vs. One-Time Purchase, Backed by Real Numbers](/articles/rork-business/rork-app-revenue-model-comparison-ads-subscription-onetime)
