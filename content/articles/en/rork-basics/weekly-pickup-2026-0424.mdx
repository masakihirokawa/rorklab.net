Thanks for spending another week with Rork Lab.

Between April 18 and 24, reader attention concentrated on one phase: getting Rork apps off the preview environment and onto real devices, then all the way to a production release. Posts about real-world friction — things that worked in preview but broke on device, platform quirks between iOS and Android, or the unglamorous release pipeline — earned noticeably longer sessions. Here are the five reads that stood out this week.

## No. 1: Rork App Works in Preview But Crashes on Device — Here's Why

Your app runs beautifully in the Rork preview, but the moment you ship it to TestFlight or install a real build, it crashes before the splash screen even clears. A lot of developers hit this wall. This guide breaks the causes apart by symptom — JS bundle issues, native dependencies, manifest permissions, release-build optimization deltas — and walks through the triage order.

I spent half a day on this exact "perfect in preview, dead on device" state with one of my wallpaper apps. The article is built around that experience, especially the sequence of what to rule out first.

[Rork App Works in Preview But Crashes on Device](/articles/rork-dev/rork-app-crashes-on-device-fix)

## No. 2: Rork App Works on iOS but Breaks on Android — A Practical Guide to Fixing Platform Differences

Rork (and React Native by extension) gives you cross-platform output out of the box, but the moment you start looking at details, iOS and Android stop agreeing with each other. This article lines up the usual platform-delta traps — status bar behavior, keyboard interaction, fonts, file paths, permission dialogs — each paired with a concrete fix.

The most annoying one in my experience: a button that only needs one tap on iOS but suddenly requires two on Android. There are a few causes, but gesture handler configuration and ripple settings are responsible most of the time, and the article walks through how to isolate which one is yours.

[Rork App Works on iOS but Breaks on Android](/articles/rork-dev/rork-ios-android-behavior-difference-fix)

## No. 3: Rork Max × AWS Amplify Gen 2 Complete Guide — Cognito Auth, AppSync GraphQL, and S3 Storage

A serious premium guide to wiring AWS Amplify Gen 2 into a Rork Max app as the production backend. Cognito for auth, AppSync for the GraphQL API, S3 for media storage — any of these take a week to get right individually. This post builds the full stack end to end.

For indie developers who default to Firebase, there are still use cases where AWS genuinely fits better, and the article starts by explaining where that judgment call lands. "Solo-built B2B2C apps aimed at enterprise-adjacent users" was the most common reader question in recent weeks, and this article was written with that audience in mind.

[Rork Max × AWS Amplify Gen 2 Complete Guide](/articles/rork-dev/rork-max-aws-amplify-gen2-complete-guide)

## No. 4: Rork × Fastlane × EAS Release Automation Guide — Ship With One Command

If your release ritual still eats an entire afternoon each time, this premium guide is built to give that afternoon back. It lays out how to combine Fastlane and EAS so screenshot generation, metadata sync, and TestFlight / Play Console distribution all run from a single command.

I used to burn every weekend doing preview recording → screenshot recapture → text updates → build → upload by hand, one update at a time. After switching to the unified pipeline described in the article, a single release now takes about thirty minutes of attention instead of a full day.

[Rork × Fastlane × EAS Release Automation Guide](/articles/rork-dev/rork-fastlane-eas-release-automation-complete-guide)

## No. 5: Using Notion as a CMS Backend for Rork Apps — Auth, Block Rendering, Rate Limits, and the 1-Hour Image URL Problem

A premium guide on treating Notion as a CMS so non-engineers can update your Rork app's content. It covers OAuth, block-level rendering, Notion API's strict rate limits, and the real operational pain point — those Notion-hosted image URLs that expire after one hour.

For solo developers, the draw of Notion as a CMS is huge: you skip building your own admin panel entirely. But the expiring image URL issue is guaranteed to bite you in production, so the article explains the caching-and-refresh pattern that actually works, with code.

[Using Notion as a CMS Backend for Rork Apps](/articles/rork-dev/rork-notion-api-cms-content-app-complete-guide)

## Looking Back at the Week

This week's dominant theme was "past preview, into production." Rork gets you to a working prototype exceptionally fast, but there's a real, not-trivial last 20% between preview and release — and that's what each of these articles chipped away at.
