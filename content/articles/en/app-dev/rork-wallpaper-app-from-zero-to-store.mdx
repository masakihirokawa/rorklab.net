Wallpaper apps are one of the better starting points for indie app developers. The core functionality is simple, the content does most of the work, and a well-made wallpaper app can stay relevant in the App Store for years without major engineering effort.

Here's the full story of building one with Rork and getting it live — the smooth parts and the frustrating ones.

## Planning — Figuring Out What Would Make It Worth Building

Before writing a single prompt, I installed and used over 30 wallpaper apps from the App Store. The pattern that stood out: most of them were essentially image dumps — large collections with minimal curation or care for the download experience.

What I wanted to build was different: a small, curated selection where the editorial judgment was the product. Ten images that someone would say "I love the taste behind this" over a hundred images with no point of view.

Locking in this direction early had a practical benefit — it kept my Rork prompts focused. "Build a clean gallery app that surfaces a small, curated collection of wallpapers" is a better brief than "build a wallpaper app with lots of images."

## Building the UI with Rork — What Worked and What Didn't

**What worked well:**

The main screen — a grid layout showing thumbnail images — is exactly the kind of UI Rork handles reliably. "Create a gallery screen with a grid layout of images, tapping opens a full-screen preview" produced a strong initial version in one prompt. A few refinements later, it was close to what I had in mind.

The detail view, where users can download the wallpaper, also came together quickly. Positioning the download button and managing the two-step flow (tap to preview, tap again to save) worked without needing to touch the generated code directly.

**What required extra work:**

The skeleton loading animation — the gray placeholder that shows while images load — was harder to adjust through prompts alone. "Make the fade-in slightly slower" didn't land consistently. I ended up editing the generated React Native code directly to get the timing right.

The wallpaper-setting UI (choosing between Home Screen, Lock Screen, or Both) ran into iOS native limitations that Rork's in-app preview couldn't fully simulate. This required real device testing with Rork Companion through multiple rounds of iteration before the behavior matched expectations.

## Preparing Image Assets — The Most Time-Consuming Part

More time went into content preparation than into the technical build. For a wallpaper app, image rights matter and require documentation.

I used a combination of my own photographs and CC0-licensed images from stock sites. All images were prepared at 3x resolution for iPhone's highest-density displays and converted to WebP to reduce file size without quality loss.

Worth noting: App Review occasionally asks about image attribution and copyright. Keeping a simple document that tracks the source and license for each image during the preparation stage saves headaches later.

## App Store Review — What Happened

The first submission was rejected. The reason: screenshots didn't meet App Store specifications. I'd submitted screenshots directly from Rork's preview, but the App Store requires screenshots at specific resolutions for different iPhone models.

I rebuilt the screenshots in Figma to the correct dimensions for each required device size and resubmitted. That version was approved.

Total time from first submission to approval: approximately 10 days, including the back-and-forth after the rejection. The timeline varies significantly by app, but treating rejection as a likely step rather than a failure makes the process easier to navigate.

## What the First Month Taught Me

The biggest download spike didn't happen at launch. It came about three weeks in, when the app started appearing higher in App Store search results.

Keyword configuration in App Store Connect matters more than it first appears. Beyond obvious terms, including compound phrases that people actually search — things like "iPhone wallpaper aesthetic" and "phone background minimalist" — expanded the range of queries the app appeared for.

The other thing that became clear: for a wallpaper app, ongoing content is the real maintenance cost. Not engineering updates — new images. Designing a simple system for adding new wallpapers on a regular schedule (weekly or monthly) from the beginning makes long-term maintenance much more sustainable.

## For Anyone Considering This

Building a wallpaper app with Rork is a particularly good fit. The core UI patterns — galleries, full-screen previews, download flows — are well within Rork's reliable range. The complexity ceiling you'll hit is modest, which means more of your energy goes into content and presentation rather than debugging.

The constraint that makes this work as an indie project: editorial focus. A small, well-chosen collection with a clear point of view will outlast a large generic one. That's true regardless of the tools you use to build it — but Rork makes it possible to build it without a team.
