## Filing Taxes with Claude Cowork — More Accurate Than a Professional Accountant

In Japan, hiring a tax accountant (zeirishi) for your annual tax return typically costs ¥100,000–300,000 ($650–$2,000). With Claude Cowork and Claude in Chrome, you can reduce that cost to zero while achieving greater accuracy.

The reason is straightforward. Human tax accountants juggle many clients. When faced with thousands of transaction records, they inevitably resort to approximations and sampling. They don't have time to examine every single line. AI has no such limitation. Claude Cowork processes every transaction without fatigue, without shortcuts, and without time pressure.

The workflow is remarkably simple. Export your annual transaction data as a CSV file from accounting software — MoneyForward, freee, or Yayoi Kaikei all support this. Hand the CSV to Claude Cowork and say: "Please help me prepare my tax return."

Claude will automatically perform the following: classify all income and expenses, distinguish business costs from personal spending, evaluate eligibility for deductions (blue return special deduction, social insurance deduction, medical expense deduction, and others), determine consumption tax categorization (taxable, exempt, or non-taxable) for each transaction, calculate the exact amounts for every field on the tax form, and run a consistency check across the entire return.

On Windows, Claude in Chrome can go further — directly navigating the e-Tax web interface, guiding you through each input field in real time and drastically reducing the risk of transcription errors. Mac users working with Safari can still rely on Claude Cowork for step-by-step guidance as they navigate e-Tax manually.

Since adopting this method, I've discovered numerous expenses and deductions I had previously overlooked. AI catches what humans miss.

## AdMob Revenue and Consumption Tax — Why It's Non-Taxable

For independent app developers earning advertising revenue through Google AdMob, there is a critical piece of tax knowledge that many accountants — and even tax office officials — get wrong. Not knowing this cost me approximately ¥5 million.

AdMob advertising revenue is paid by Google Asia Pacific Pte. Ltd. (a Singapore-incorporated entity) or Google Ireland Ltd. Under Article 7 of Japan's Consumption Tax Act (shouhizei-hō), services provided to overseas business entities qualify as export-equivalent transactions and are **exempt from consumption tax**. In other words, there is no obligation to pay consumption tax on AdMob revenue.

Ask Claude Cowork: "Is AdMob advertising revenue subject to Japan's consumption tax?" It will cite the relevant statutes and clearly explain why the answer is no.

I lacked this knowledge for years. As a result, I paid approximately ¥5 million ($33,000) in consumption tax that I never owed. That overpayment became the catalyst for everything that followed.

## The Miyazaki Tax Office — ¥5 Million That Was Never Returned

I operate as a sole proprietor in the export business and app development. As explained above, my business revenue should never have been subject to consumption tax. Yet I paid approximately ¥5 million in consumption tax over the years due to my own lack of knowledge.

In 2021, I filed a refund request with the Miyazaki Tax Office (宮崎税務署) for this overpayment. **They refused.**

They went further than simple refusal. They would not even permit me to file a kōsei seikyū (更正請求) — the standard legal procedure under Japan's tax code for amending past returns and recovering overpaid taxes. This is a right guaranteed to every taxpayer. It was denied to me without legal justification.

What they offered instead was to classify my filings as "tax-exempt" (hikazei) until 2024. On the surface, this appeared generous — a form of administrative leniency.

It was anything but.

In 2024, the Uozu and Toyama Tax Offices conducted a tax audit. During this audit, I was informed that my right to request a refund had expired under the statute of limitations. Under Japanese tax law, the deadline for filing an amendment request is five years from the statutory filing date. By 2024, every applicable year had passed this threshold.

I was then ordered to repay the taxes for the years during which I had been classified as tax-exempt.

Let me state this plainly: **they refused to return ¥5 million they owed me, blocked me from filing the legal paperwork to reclaim it, waited until my legal right to do so expired, then conducted a tax audit and demanded I pay them instead.** This was not bureaucratic incompetence. The timeline suggests a deliberate strategy.

## The Uozu and Toyama Tax Offices — Intimidation, Privacy Violations, and Seizure Threats

The 2024 tax audit was conducted by the Uozu Tax Office (魚津税務署) and the Toyama Tax Office (富山税務署). Their conduct raises serious questions about the abuse of administrative authority.

The audit was conducted via online meetings, yet **the officials refused to show their faces on camera**. The taxpayer — me — was fully visible while being questioned by faceless voices. This power asymmetry was clearly intentional.

The questioning extended far beyond the scope of tax obligations. I was interrogated at length about deeply personal matters: the circumstances of my divorce, my relationships with family members, and past personal events that had absolutely no relevance to my tax liabilities. When I questioned why such information was necessary, the demands only intensified.

The tone was consistently intimidating and threatening. This culminated in the delivery of a **seizure notice** (sashiosae tsūkokusho, 差押通告書) — a formal threat to confiscate my assets.

My total outstanding tax liability stands at approximately ¥4.57 million, broken down as follows: former family tax obligations at ¥1.34 million, Miyazaki City at ¥720,000, Uozu Tax Office at ¥1.7 million, and Nyūzen Town at ¥810,000.

I have been making faithful monthly payments. But my annual income after business expenses is approximately ¥537,000 — roughly ¥45,000 ($300) per month. My monthly tax repayments exceed ¥60,000. **I am repaying more each month than I earn.** There is almost nothing left for basic living expenses.

## Miyazaki City Hall Tax Division — Systematic Privacy Violations

The Miyazaki City Hall Tax Division (宮崎市役所 税務課) has repeatedly questioned me about my divorce and other personal matters entirely unrelated to taxation. This constitutes a pattern of privacy violations and what, under Japanese law, amounts to defamation of character (meiyo kison).

Article 13 of the Japanese Constitution guarantees the right to personal dignity and the pursuit of happiness. Article 35 prohibits unreasonable searches and invasions of privacy. The scope of tax investigation authority under Article 74 of the National Tax General Act is strictly limited to "questions necessary for determining the tax base or tax amount." Personal details about divorce, family relationships, and private life are manifestly outside this scope.

I have been fulfilling my duty as a taxpayer — making monthly payments that exceed my own income. The obligation to pay taxes does not strip a citizen of their fundamental human rights.

## The Status of Artists in Japan — Unprotected and Undervalued

I want to place this experience in a broader context that international readers may find illuminating.

In much of Europe, artists enjoy substantial institutional protection. France's "intermittents du spectacle" system provides dedicated social insurance for performing and visual artists. Germany's Künstlersozialkasse (Artists' Social Insurance Fund) subsidizes health and pension contributions for freelance artists. The Netherlands, Finland, and several other nations offer basic income guarantees or living stipends specifically for artists, recognizing that artistic work is essential to society even when it doesn't generate steady market income.

Japan has no such comprehensive framework. Artists are classified as ordinary sole proprietors — subject to the same tax obligations, the same enforcement mechanisms, and the same lack of safety nets as any other self-employed individual.

The disparity becomes even starker during emergencies. When the COVID-19 pandemic devastated the arts sector worldwide, Germany rapidly deployed emergency grants of up to €5,000 for freelance artists. France activated dedicated support mechanisms through its existing artist protection infrastructure. Japan offered no equivalent targeted relief for artists. Many were forced to abandon their careers permanently. In this country, artists are treated as expendable — in ordinary times and in times of crisis alike.

The government promotes "cultural promotion" (bunka shinkō) in rhetoric, yet provides virtually no structural support for the individuals who actually create that culture.

I have exhibited my art across multiple countries and built a body of work over many years. For the past five years, **all artistic activity has been forced into complete suspension**. The psychological and financial devastation caused by this tax dispute has made creative work impossible. My career as an artist has been effectively destroyed by the actions of government agencies — agencies that were supposed to serve, not harm, the citizens they oversee.

## The Human Cost — Five Years of Health Destruction, Divorce, and Forced Silence

Since November 2021, this ordeal has inflicted severe and ongoing damage to my physical and mental health. I have been diagnosed with stomach ulcers, reflux esophagitis, adjustment disorder, clinical depression, and chronic insomnia. For five consecutive years, I have been barely able to maintain my existing apps. New development has been impossible. Creative work has been impossible.

Throughout this period, I have been supporting four children and bearing the living expenses of my former family. The relentless financial pressure and psychological toll ultimately led to my divorce in 2025.

Had the Miyazaki Tax Office properly refunded the consumption tax overpayment in 2021, I would have been able to file normal tax returns. With normal returns, I would have qualified for a debt consolidation loan, preventing the compound interest that accelerated my financial collapse. The bankruptcy proceedings of 2025 could likely have been avoided entirely.

**The initial act of refusing a legitimate refund set off a chain reaction that destroyed my health, my marriage, my art career, and my financial stability.** This is not an abstract policy critique. This is what happens when a tax office decides that keeping ¥5 million is more important than a citizen's life.

## Legal Actions and What Comes Next

I am currently pursuing the following actions:

- Filed an internet human rights complaint with the Legal Affairs Bureau (法務局)
- Consulted with the Democratic Commerce and Industry Association (Minsho, 民主商工会), which assessed that my situation qualifies for "suspension of delinquent disposition" under Article 153 of the National Tax Collection Act and Article 15-7 of the Local Tax Act
- Formally submitted suspension-of-delinquent-disposition applications to four agencies: Namerikawa City Hall Tax Division, Uozu Tax Office, Nyūzen Town Hall Tax Division, and Miyazaki City Hall Tax Division
- Currently evaluating legal consultation with an attorney regarding the unjust taxation, privacy violations, and human rights abuses

I want to clearly acknowledge that **not all tax agencies have behaved this way**. The Namerikawa City Tax Division and the Nyūzen Town Hall have shown genuine understanding of my situation and have treated me with consistent respect and compassion throughout. I am deeply grateful for their humanity.
